Chapter Overview

Understanding our environment and the delicate balance that sustains life on Earth.

What is Environment?

The environment refers to the sum total of all the external conditions and influences that affect the life and development of organisms. It includes everything that surrounds us — the air we breathe, the water we drink, the soil beneath our feet, and all living organisms around us.

Our environment comprises two main components:

  • Biotic components — All living organisms: plants, animals, microorganisms, fungi, bacteria
  • Abiotic components — All non-living physical and chemical factors: temperature, rainfall, wind, soil, minerals, light, water, humidity

Studying the environment is critical because human activities have been altering natural systems at an alarming rate. Understanding how ecosystems function helps us protect biodiversity, prevent pollution, and ensure sustainable development for future generations.

Ecosystem and Its Components

A self-sustaining unit of nature where living and non-living things interact.

What is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is a self-sustaining functional unit of nature where living organisms (biotic community) interact with each other and with their physical environment (abiotic factors). It includes all the organisms in a given area along with their non-living environment.

Types of Ecosystems

  • Natural ecosystems: Forests, ponds, lakes, oceans, grasslands, deserts, wetlands — formed and maintained by nature without human intervention.
  • Artificial (man-made) ecosystems: Gardens, crop fields, aquariums, artificial ponds — created and maintained by humans.

Abiotic Components

The non-living physical and chemical factors that influence living organisms:

  • Climatic factors: Temperature, rainfall, humidity, wind, light
  • Edaphic factors: Soil type, pH, mineral content, organic matter
  • Other factors: Water availability, atmospheric gases, topography

Biotic Components — Functional Groups

1. Producers (Autotrophs)

Organisms that can synthesise their own food from inorganic substances using sunlight energy. They form the base of every food chain.

  • Examples: Green plants, algae, phytoplankton, cyanobacteria
  • Process: Photosynthesis — converting CO₂ and H₂O into glucose using sunlight
6CO₂ + 6H₂O ⟶ C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ Sunlight + Chlorophyll

2. Consumers (Heterotrophs)

Organisms that cannot make their own food and depend directly or indirectly on producers:

  • Primary consumers (Herbivores): Feed directly on plants — deer, rabbit, grasshopper, cow, caterpillar
  • Secondary consumers (Small carnivores): Feed on herbivores — frog, snake, fox, small birds
  • Tertiary consumers (Top carnivores): Feed on secondary consumers — lion, eagle, shark, tiger
  • Omnivores: Feed on both plants and animals — bear, human, crow, pig
  • Parasites: Live on or in a host and derive nutrition from it — tapeworm, Cuscuta, lice

3. Decomposers (Saprotrophs)

Organisms that break down dead organic matter (dead plants, animals, faecal matter) into simpler inorganic substances. They play a crucial role in nutrient recycling, returning minerals and nutrients back to the soil and atmosphere.

  • Examples: Bacteria, fungi (mushrooms, moulds), earthworms
  • Importance: Without decomposers, dead organisms would accumulate, nutrients would be locked up, and the ecosystem would collapse.
Fun Fact

A single teaspoon of fertile soil contains more microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, protozoa) than there are people on Earth! These decomposers work tirelessly to recycle nutrients, making the soil rich for plant growth.

Food Chains and Food Webs

Tracing the path of energy and nutrients through living organisms.

Food Chain

A food chain is a linear sequence of organisms through which nutrients and energy pass as one organism eats another. Each step in the food chain is called a trophic level.

Examples of Food Chains

Grassland: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle
Forest: Grass → Deer → Lion
Aquatic: Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Small Fish → Large Fish → Bird

Trophic Levels

  • T1 — Producers: Green plants, algae (make their own food)
  • T2 — Primary Consumers: Herbivores (eat producers)
  • T3 — Secondary Consumers: Small carnivores (eat herbivores)
  • T4 — Tertiary Consumers: Top carnivores (eat secondary consumers)

Note: Decomposers operate at every trophic level, breaking down dead organisms from all levels.

Energy Flow in Ecosystem

Understanding the 10% law and why energy flow is unidirectional.

The 10% Law (Lindeman's Law)

According to Lindeman's 10% Law (1942), only about 10% of the energy available at one trophic level is transferred to the next trophic level. The remaining 90% is used by the organism for its own life processes (respiration, movement, reproduction) or lost as heat to the environment.

Producers capture 1000 J → Herbivores get 100 J → Secondary consumers get 10 J → Tertiary consumers get 1 J Only 10% of energy is passed to the next level

Key Points about Energy Flow

  • Unidirectional: Energy flows in only one direction — from sun to producers to consumers. It cannot flow backwards from consumers to producers.
  • Non-cyclical: Unlike nutrients, energy does not cycle through the ecosystem. It enters as sunlight and exits as heat.
  • Maximum 4-5 trophic levels: Because so much energy is lost at each level, very little remains after 4-5 transfers, making longer food chains impossible.
  • Maximum biomass at producer level: The total amount of living matter (biomass) is greatest at the producer level and decreases at each successive trophic level (pyramid of biomass).

Why Are Food Chains Short?

Food chains are generally limited to 3-5 trophic levels because of the enormous energy loss at each step. If producers capture 10,000 J of solar energy, only 10 J would be available at the fourth trophic level — barely enough to sustain life. This progressive energy loss is the fundamental reason food chains cannot be infinitely long.

Fun Fact

Green plants capture only about 1% of total solar energy falling on their leaves through photosynthesis. Of this captured energy, only 10% passes to herbivores. So a top predator receives just 0.001% of the original solar energy!

Biological Magnification

How non-biodegradable toxins concentrate up the food chain.

What is Biomagnification?

Biological magnification (biomagnification) is the progressive increase in the concentration of non-biodegradable substances (like pesticides, heavy metals) at each trophic level of a food chain. Organisms at higher trophic levels accumulate greater concentrations because they consume many organisms from the lower level, each containing the toxin.

How It Occurs

  1. Pesticides like DDT are sprayed on crops and enter water bodies through runoff.
  2. Plankton absorb DDT from the water (very low concentration: ~0.003 ppm).
  3. Small fish eat many plankton, accumulating more DDT (~0.5 ppm).
  4. Large fish eat many small fish, concentrating DDT further (~2 ppm).
  5. Birds/Humans at the top accumulate the highest concentration (~25 ppm).

Impact

  • DDT caused eggshell thinning in birds of prey (like the bald eagle), nearly driving them to extinction.
  • Top predators (including humans) are most affected — risks include cancer, organ damage, hormonal disruption, and immune system damage.
  • Non-biodegradable substances that magnify: DDT, heavy metals (mercury, lead, cadmium), PCBs, plastics.
  • Biodegradable substances do NOT magnify because they are broken down by decomposers at each level.

Ozone Layer Depletion

Earth's protective shield and the threat posed by CFCs.

The Ozone Layer

Ozone (O₃) is a molecule made of three oxygen atoms. It is found primarily in the stratosphere, about 15-60 km above Earth's surface. This layer of ozone acts as a shield, absorbing and blocking most of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet (UV-B and UV-C) radiation from reaching Earth's surface.

Formation of Ozone

Step 1: O₂ ⟶ O + O UV radiation splits oxygen molecule into free oxygen atoms
Step 2: O + O₂ → O₃ Free oxygen atom combines with O₂ to form ozone

This process of formation and breakdown of ozone is a continuous, balanced cycle in the stratosphere — the ozone-oxygen cycle.

Cause of Ozone Depletion: CFCs

Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are synthetic chemicals that were widely used in refrigerators, air conditioners, aerosol sprays, fire extinguishers, and foam manufacturing. Once released, CFCs rise to the stratosphere where UV radiation breaks them apart.

How CFCs Destroy Ozone

Step 1: CFC ⟶ Cl + other fragments UV radiation breaks CFC, releasing chlorine (Cl) atom
Step 2: Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂ Chlorine attacks ozone, forming chlorine monoxide and oxygen
Step 3: ClO + O → Cl + O₂ Chlorine is regenerated — 1 Cl atom can destroy thousands of O₃ molecules!

The Ozone Hole

In 1985, scientists discovered a severe thinning of the ozone layer over Antarctica, commonly called the "ozone hole." This alarming discovery prompted global action.

International Response: Montreal Protocol

The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) facilitated the Montreal Protocol in 1987 — an international agreement to phase out the production and use of CFCs and other ozone-depleting substances. It is considered one of the most successful environmental treaties in history, and the ozone layer is slowly recovering.

Effects of Ozone Depletion

Without the ozone shield, harmful UV radiation reaches Earth's surface, causing:

  • Skin cancer (melanoma and other types)
  • Cataracts and other eye damage
  • Weakened immune system
  • Damage to phytoplankton (base of aquatic food chains)
  • Reduced crop yields and harm to plant growth
  • Disruption of aquatic ecosystems

Waste Management

Biodegradable vs non-biodegradable waste and strategies for managing them.

Classification of Waste

Biodegradable Waste

Substances that can be broken down by biological processes (by bacteria, fungi, and other decomposers) into simpler, harmless substances within a reasonable time.

  • Examples: Fruit and vegetable peels, paper, cotton, wood, food waste, cow dung, fallen leaves, dead organisms
  • Outcome: Decomposed into compost/humus, nutrients recycled back into soil

Non-biodegradable Waste

Substances that cannot be broken down by biological processes and persist in the environment for very long periods (hundreds to thousands of years).

  • Examples: Plastics, glass, metals (aluminium cans), DDT, radioactive waste, synthetic fibres (polyester, nylon), electronic waste
  • Problems: Biomagnification, soil and water pollution, health hazards, clogged drains and waterways, harm to wildlife (animals ingest plastic)

Waste Management Strategies

The 3 Rs

  • Reduce: Minimise the amount of waste produced — avoid unnecessary purchases, use less packaging, carry reusable bags.
  • Reuse: Use items again instead of discarding them — refill water bottles, donate old clothes, use containers for storage.
  • Recycle: Convert waste materials into new products — paper recycling, metal recycling, plastic recycling (where possible).

Other Strategies

  • Composting / Vermicomposting: Biodegradable waste is decomposed by microorganisms or earthworms to form compost (natural fertiliser).
  • Segregation at source: Separating wet (biodegradable) and dry (non-biodegradable) waste at the household level.
  • Proper disposal of hazardous waste: E-waste, batteries, medical waste need specialised treatment.
  • Plastic reduction: Banning single-use plastics, using biodegradable alternatives.
Fun Fact

A plastic bag can take 500-1000 years to decompose, while a banana peel breaks down in just 2-5 weeks! India generates over 26,000 tonnes of plastic waste every day, making proper waste management a critical environmental challenge.
