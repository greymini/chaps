Chapter Overview

Understanding how substances transform and how we represent these changes using chemical equations.

What Are Chemical Reactions?

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances (called reactants) are transformed into new substances (called products) with different chemical properties. Unlike physical changes (such as melting ice), chemical reactions involve the breaking and forming of chemical bonds, creating entirely new substances.

Chemical changes are permanent and usually cannot be easily reversed. They are different from physical changes in fundamental ways — the composition of the substance changes at the molecular level.

How to Identify a Chemical Reaction

You can tell a chemical reaction has occurred by observing one or more of these signs:

🎨

Change in Colour

e.g., black CuO turns brown Cu when heated with H₂.

🌡️

Change in Temperature

Heat is released (exothermic) or absorbed (endothermic).

💨

Gas Evolution

Bubbles form, e.g., Zn + HCl produces H₂ gas.

☂️

Precipitate Formation

An insoluble solid forms, e.g., BaSO₄ precipitate.

⚡

Change in State

Solid to liquid, liquid to gas, etc., during a reaction.

Section 2: Chemical Equations

The language chemists use to represent chemical reactions concisely and precisely.

From Words to Symbols

Word Equation

A chemical reaction can first be written in words:

Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide

Chemical Equation (Skeletal)

Replace the names with chemical formulae to get a skeletal equation:

Mg + O₂ → MgO (Unbalanced — skeletal equation)

Balanced Chemical Equation

According to the Law of Conservation of Mass, atoms can be neither created nor destroyed. The number of atoms of each element must be the same on both sides. We balance the equation by adjusting coefficients:

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (Balanced: 2 Mg and 2 O on each side)

How to Balance Equations: Hit-and-Trial Method

The hit-and-trial method involves adjusting coefficients step by step until the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides. Follow these steps:

  1. Write the skeletal equation with correct formulae.
  2. List the number of atoms of each element on both sides.
  3. Start balancing the element that appears in the fewest formulae (often a metal or the most complex compound).
  4. Adjust coefficients (never change subscripts in formulae).
  5. Repeat until all elements are balanced.
  6. Verify by counting atoms on both sides.

Worked Example: Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Step 1: List atom counts for the skeletal equation:

Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂ Fe: 1 vs 3 | H: 2 vs 2 | O: 1 vs 4

Step 2: Balance Fe by placing coefficient 3 before Fe:

3Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂ Fe: 3 = 3 ✓ | O: 1 vs 4 | H: 2 vs 2

Step 3: Balance O by placing coefficient 4 before H₂O:

3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂ Fe: 3 = 3 ✓ | O: 4 = 4 ✓ | H: 8 vs 2

Step 4: Balance H by placing coefficient 4 before H₂:

3Fe(s) + 4H₂O(g) → Fe₃O₄(s) + 4H₂(g) Fe: 3 = 3 ✓ | O: 4 = 4 ✓ | H: 8 = 8 ✓ — Balanced!

Fun Fact

Antoine Lavoisier, the "Father of Modern Chemistry," established the Law of Conservation of Mass in 1789. He carefully measured masses before and after reactions, proving that matter is never created or destroyed — it only changes form. This is why we must balance chemical equations!

Types of Reactions

1. Combination Reaction

A reaction in which two or more substances combine to form a single product. Also called a synthesis reaction.

General form: A + B → AB

Example 1: Quicklime and Water

When calcium oxide (quicklime) reacts with water, it forms calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) and releases heat. This is an exothermic reaction used in whitewashing.

CaO(s) + H₂O(l) → Ca(OH)₂(aq) Quicklime + Water → Slaked lime (Exothermic)

Example 2: Burning Magnesium

When a magnesium ribbon is burned in air, it combines with oxygen to form magnesium oxide — a white powder. The reaction produces a dazzling white flame.

2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s) Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide (Dazzling white flame)
Fun Fact

The heat produced when quicklime reacts with water is so intense that it can actually boil the water! Builders use this reaction for whitewashing walls — the Ca(OH)₂ slowly reacts with CO₂ in air to form a hard white layer of CaCO₃.

2. Decomposition Reaction

A reaction in which a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. It is the opposite of a combination reaction and usually requires energy input.

General form: AB → A + B

Types of Decomposition

a) Thermal Decomposition (by heat)

2FeSO₄(s) ⟶ Fe₂O₃(s) + SO₂(g) + SO₃(g) Ferrous sulphate (green) → Ferric oxide (brown) + gases (Δ heat)
CaCO₃(s) ⟶ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) Limestone → Quicklime + Carbon dioxide (Δ heat)

b) Electrolytic Decomposition (by electricity)

2H₂O(l) ⟶ 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) Water → Hydrogen + Oxygen (Electrolysis)

c) Photolytic Decomposition (by light)

Photolytic decomposition is caused by sunlight or UV light. This is why silver chloride and silver bromide are stored in dark-coloured bottles.

2AgCl(s) ⟶ 2Ag(s) + Cl₂(g) Silver chloride → Silver + Chlorine (Sunlight)
2AgBr(s) ⟶ 2Ag(s) + Br₂(g) Silver bromide → Silver + Bromine (Sunlight)

Decomposition: Three Types Illustrated

3. Displacement Reaction

A reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. The reactivity of metals follows the reactivity series.

General form: A + BC → AC + B (where A is more reactive than B)

Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s) Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate (blue → green)
Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s) Zinc displaces copper (Zn is more reactive than Cu)

Displacement: Iron in Copper Sulphate

4. Double Displacement Reaction

A reaction in which two compounds exchange their ions to form two new compounds. This often results in a precipitate, a gas, or water.

General form: AB + CD → AD + CB

Na₂SO₄(aq) + BaCl₂(aq) → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl(aq) White precipitate of BaSO₄ forms (Precipitation reaction)
NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) Neutralisation reaction: Acid + Base → Salt + Water

5. Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) Reactions

Reactions involving the transfer of oxygen or hydrogen between substances. In a redox reaction, oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously.

Key Definitions

  • Oxidation — Gain of oxygen OR loss of hydrogen by a substance.
  • Reduction — Loss of oxygen OR gain of hydrogen by a substance.
  • Oxidising agent — The substance that gets reduced (provides oxygen or accepts hydrogen).
  • Reducing agent — The substance that gets oxidised (accepts oxygen or provides hydrogen).
CuO(s) + H₂(g) → Cu(s) + H₂O(l) CuO is reduced (loses O) | H₂ is oxidised (gains O)

Here, CuO is the oxidising agent (it provides oxygen to H₂) and H₂ is the reducing agent (it removes oxygen from CuO).

ZnO(s) + C(s) → Zn(s) + CO(g) ZnO is reduced | C is oxidised

Section 4: Effects of Oxidation in Everyday Life

How oxidation affects metals and food in our daily lives.

Corrosion

Corrosion is the process of gradual destruction of metals by chemical reactions with their environment (usually oxygen and moisture). It is essentially the oxidation of metals in the presence of air and water.

Iron Rusting

When iron is exposed to moist air, it forms a reddish-brown flaky substance called rust (hydrated iron(III) oxide).

4Fe(s) + 3O₂(g) + xH₂O(l) → 2Fe₂O₃·xH₂O(s) Iron + Oxygen + Water → Rust (Hydrated ferric oxide)

Rust is porous and flaky, so it does not protect the underlying iron. It gradually eats away the entire object. Prevention methods: painting, oiling, greasing, galvanising (coating with zinc), chrome-plating, alloying (e.g., stainless steel).

Copper Corrosion

Copper reacts with moist CO₂ in air to form a green coating of basic copper carbonate.

2Cu + H₂O + CO₂ + O₂ → Cu(OH)₂·CuCO₃ Copper → Green coating (Basic copper carbonate)

Silver Tarnishing

Silver reacts with H₂S in air to form a black coating of silver sulphide.

2Ag(s) + H₂S(g) → Ag₂S(s) + H₂(g) Silver → Black coating (Silver sulphide)

Rancidity

Rancidity is the oxidation of fats and oils in food, causing an unpleasant smell and taste. When food containing fats and oils is exposed to air, the fats are slowly oxidised, making the food unfit for consumption.

Prevention of Rancidity

  • Adding antioxidants — Substances like BHA (butylated hydroxyanisole) and BHT are added to food to prevent oxidation of fats.
  • Nitrogen atmosphere — Chip packets are flushed with nitrogen gas to prevent oxidation. Nitrogen is unreactive and displaces oxygen.
  • Refrigeration — Low temperatures slow down the rate of oxidation.
  • Airtight containers — Preventing contact between food and air (oxygen).
  • Vacuum packing — Removing air from packaging entirely.
Did You Know?

The Statue of Liberty is actually made of copper, but its famous green colour is due to corrosion! Over time, the copper has reacted with moist air to form a patina of copper carbonate. Unlike rust on iron, this green layer actually protects the copper underneath from further corrosion.

Chemical Equations

  • Word equation → skeletal equation → balanced equation.
  • Must obey the Law of Conservation of Mass.
  • State symbols: (s), (l), (g), (aq), ↑, ↓, Δ.

Combination & Decomposition

  • Combination: two or more substances form one product.
  • Decomposition: one compound splits (needs energy: heat, electricity, or light).
  • They are opposite reactions.

Displacement Reactions

  • More reactive element displaces less reactive element.
  • Based on the reactivity series of metals.
  • Double displacement: ion exchange, often forms precipitate.

Redox & Everyday Effects

  • Oxidation = gain of O or loss of H.
  • Reduction = loss of O or gain of H.
  • Corrosion: metals + O₂ + H₂O (rusting, tarnishing).
  • Rancidity: oxidation of fats in food.

Key Terms

Chemical reaction — transformation of reactants into products
Balanced equation — equal atoms of each element on both sides
Combination — two or more substances form one product
Decomposition — one compound breaks into simpler substances
Displacement — more reactive element replaces less reactive
Double displacement — exchange of ions between compounds
Precipitate — insoluble solid formed in a reaction
Oxidation — gain of oxygen or loss of hydrogen
Reduction — loss of oxygen or gain of hydrogen
Redox — reaction where oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously
Corrosion — slow oxidation of metals by air and moisture
Rancidity — oxidation of fats causing bad smell/taste
Exothermic — reaction that releases heat
Endothermic — reaction that absorbs heat
Galvanisation — coating iron with zinc to prevent rusting
