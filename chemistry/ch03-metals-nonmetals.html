Chapter Overview

Understanding the classification, properties, and chemistry of metals and non-metals.

What Are Metals and Non-metals?

Elements can be broadly classified as metals and non-metals based on their physical and chemical properties. In the modern periodic table, metals are found on the left side and centre, while non-metals occupy the upper right side. A staircase-like line separates them, with metalloids (like boron, silicon, germanium) found along this boundary, showing properties of both.

Out of the 118 known elements, about 80 are metals. Common metals include iron, copper, aluminium, gold, silver, and sodium. Common non-metals include carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, sulphur, and chlorine.

Section 2: Physical Properties

Comparing the physical characteristics that distinguish metals from non-metals.

Properties of Metals

  • Lustre — Metals have a characteristic shiny appearance (metallic lustre) due to the reflection of light from their smooth surface.
  • Malleability — Metals can be hammered or rolled into thin sheets without breaking. Gold and silver are the most malleable metals.
  • Ductility — Metals can be drawn into thin wires. Gold is the most ductile metal; 1 g of gold can be drawn into a wire 2 km long.
  • Conductivity — Metals are excellent conductors of heat and electricity due to free-moving (delocalised) electrons. Silver is the best conductor, followed by copper and gold.
  • Sonority — Metals produce a ringing sound when struck (sonorous). This property makes them useful in bells and musical instruments.
  • State — Metals are generally solid at room temperature. Exception: Mercury (Hg) is a liquid metal.
  • Melting/Boiling points — Generally high. Exceptions: Sodium (Na) and potassium (K) have low melting points and are soft enough to be cut with a knife.
  • Density — Generally high. Exceptions: Lithium, sodium, and potassium are light enough to float on water.

Properties of Non-metals

  • Lustre — Non-metals are generally dull in appearance. Exceptions: Iodine has a lustrous surface; diamond sparkles brilliantly.
  • Malleability & Ductility — Non-metals are brittle; they break or crumble when hammered or stretched.
  • Conductivity — Non-metals are poor conductors of heat and electricity. Exception: Graphite (a form of carbon) is a good conductor of electricity.
  • Sonority — Non-metals are not sonorous (they do not produce a ringing sound).
  • State — Non-metals exist in all three states: solids (sulphur, phosphorus), liquids (bromine), and gases (oxygen, nitrogen).
  • Melting/Boiling points — Generally low (except diamond and graphite).

Section 3: Chemical Properties of Metals

How metals react with oxygen, water, acids, and salt solutions.

Reaction with Oxygen

Most metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides, which are generally basic in nature (they turn red litmus blue and react with acids to form salt + water).

4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O Sodium burns vigorously with a bright golden-yellow flame
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO Magnesium burns with a dazzling white flame

The reactivity of metals with oxygen varies greatly. Sodium and potassium react so vigorously that they are stored in kerosene oil. Iron reacts slowly, while gold and platinum do not react with oxygen at all.

Amphoteric Oxides

Some metal oxides are amphoteric — they show both acidic and basic behaviour. They react with both acids and bases.

Al₂O₃ + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂O (acts as a base)
Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO₂ + H₂O (acts as an acid)

Examples of amphoteric oxides: Al₂O₃ (aluminium oxide) and ZnO (zinc oxide).

Reaction with Water

Different metals show different reactivity with water, ranging from vigorous to no reaction at all:

2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑ Very vigorous; sodium dashes across the water surface, may catch fire
2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂↑ Extremely vigorous; potassium catches fire immediately with a lilac flame
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂↑ Reacts with cold water; calcium starts floating due to H₂ gas bubbles

Magnesium reacts very slowly with cold water but reacts vigorously with hot water/steam. Iron reacts only with steam (not cold or hot water).

3Fe + 4H₂O(steam) → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂↑

Copper (Cu), silver (Ag), and gold (Au) do not react with water at all.

Reaction with Dilute Acids

Reactive metals displace hydrogen from dilute acids (HCl, H₂SO₄) to form a salt and hydrogen gas:

Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑ Zinc reacts readily; hydrogen gas pops with a burning splint
2Al + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂↑
Fe + H₂SO₄ → FeSO₄ + H₂↑

Copper (Cu), silver (Ag), and gold (Au) do not react with dilute acids because they are below hydrogen in the reactivity series.

Important Note

HNO₃ (nitric acid) is an oxidising acid and behaves differently. It does not produce H₂ gas; instead, it releases nitrogen oxides (NO₂). However, Mg and Mn react with very dilute HNO₃ to evolve H₂ gas. Aqua regia (3 parts conc. HCl + 1 part conc. HNO₃) can dissolve gold and platinum.

Reaction with Salt Solutions (Displacement)

A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution:

Fe + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s) Iron nail dipped in blue CuSO₄ solution → solution turns green, copper deposits on nail
Cu + 2AgNO₃(aq) → Cu(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2Ag(s) Copper displaces silver from silver nitrate solution

This principle is the basis of the reactivity series: metals are arranged in decreasing order of their ability to displace other metals from their salt solutions.

Section 4: Ionic Bonding

How metals and non-metals combine through electron transfer to form ionic compounds.

Formation of Ionic Bonds

When a metal reacts with a non-metal, the metal atom loses electrons (to achieve noble gas configuration) and becomes a positively charged ion (cation). The non-metal atom gains those electrons and becomes a negatively charged ion (anion). The strong electrostatic attraction between opposite ions forms an ionic bond.

Formation of Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

Na (2, 8, 1) → Na⁺ (2, 8) + e⁻ Sodium loses 1 electron to achieve stable octet
Cl (2, 8, 7) + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (2, 8, 8) Chlorine gains 1 electron to achieve stable octet

The Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are held together by strong electrostatic attraction, forming NaCl (an ionic compound).

Formation of Magnesium Chloride (MgCl₂)

Mg (2, 8, 2) → Mg²⁺ (2, 8) + 2e⁻ Magnesium loses 2 electrons
2Cl + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻ Two chlorine atoms each gain 1 electron

One Mg²⁺ ion bonds with two Cl⁻ ions to form MgCl₂.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

  • Physical state — Ionic compounds are solid at room temperature and exist as an orderly arrangement of ions called a crystal lattice.
  • High melting and boiling points — Due to strong electrostatic forces of attraction between ions, a large amount of energy is needed to break the lattice.
  • Electrical conductivity — Ionic compounds do not conduct electricity in solid state (ions are fixed). They conduct electricity when molten or dissolved in water because ions become free to move.
  • Solubility — Generally soluble in water (polar solvent) but insoluble in organic solvents like kerosene, petrol.

Section 5: Occurrence & Extraction

How metals are found in nature and extracted from their ores.

Key Definitions

  • Mineral — A naturally occurring substance containing one or more metals in combined form (oxides, sulphides, carbonates, etc.).
  • Ore — A mineral from which a metal can be profitably extracted. All ores are minerals, but not all minerals are ores. For example, clay (Al₂O₃.2SiO₂.2H₂O) is a mineral of aluminium but not an ore; bauxite (Al₂O₃.2H₂O) is the ore.
  • Gangue — The unwanted impurities (sand, rocky material, earthy particles) present in the ore.

Extraction Based on Reactivity

The method of extraction depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series:

1. Highly Reactive Metals (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al)

These metals are strong reducing agents and cannot be reduced by carbon. They are obtained by electrolytic reduction (electrolysis of their molten chlorides or oxides).

2Al₂O₃(molten) → 4Al + 3O₂ At cathode: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (electrolysis, Hall-Heroult process)

2. Moderately Reactive Metals (Zn, Fe, Pb, Cu)

These metals are present as sulphides or carbonates. They are first converted to oxides, then reduced with carbon:

Roasting (for sulphide ores — heating in excess air):

2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂ Zinc sulphide ore is roasted to zinc oxide

Calcination (for carbonate ores — heating in limited air):

ZnCO₃ → ZnO + CO₂ Zinc carbonate is calcined to zinc oxide

Reduction with carbon:

ZnO + C → Zn + CO Carbon acts as a reducing agent

3. Least Reactive Metals (Cu, Hg, Ag, Au)

These metals occur in free/native state or as sulphides. They can be obtained by just heating in air (self-reduction):

2HgS + 3O₂ → 2HgO + 2SO₂
2HgO → 2Hg + O₂ Mercury oxide decomposes on further heating

Thermite Reaction

A highly exothermic reaction where aluminium reduces iron(III) oxide:

Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → 2Fe + Al₂O₃ + Heat Produces molten iron; used to join railway tracks and repair heavy machinery

This reaction produces so much heat that the iron produced is in a molten state. It is a real-world application of the fact that aluminium is more reactive than iron.

Electrolytic Refining

Impure metals are purified using electrolysis:

  • Anode — Impure metal (thick rod)
  • Cathode — Pure metal (thin strip)
  • Electrolyte — Acidified solution of the metal salt

During electrolysis, pure metal from the anode dissolves into the solution and deposits on the cathode. Impurities settle at the bottom as anode mud.

Fun Fact

The thermite reaction produces temperatures of about 2500°C — hot enough to melt iron! This reaction was historically used to weld railway tracks in remote locations where no electricity was available. The process is called thermite welding.

Section 6: Corrosion & Prevention

Understanding how metals deteriorate and how we protect them.

What is Corrosion?

Corrosion is the gradual deterioration of a metal by the action of air, moisture, and chemicals on its surface. It is essentially a surface phenomenon.

  • Rusting of iron — Iron reacts with oxygen and moisture to form hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust): Fe₂O₃.xH₂O. Rust is reddish-brown, flaky, and does not protect the underlying iron.
  • Tarnishing of silver — Silver reacts with H₂S in air to form a black coating of silver sulphide (Ag₂S).
  • Green coating on copper — Copper reacts with CO₂ and moisture to form green copper carbonate: CuCO₃.Cu(OH)₂.

Note: Aluminium forms a thin oxide layer (Al₂O₃) that is protective and prevents further corrosion, unlike iron rust.

Prevention of Corrosion

  • Painting — Applying a coat of paint prevents contact with air and moisture.
  • Oiling and greasing — Applied to machinery parts to prevent rusting.
  • Galvanising — Coating iron with a thin layer of zinc. Even if the zinc layer is scratched, zinc (being more reactive) preferentially corrodes, protecting the iron underneath (sacrificial protection).
  • Chrome plating — Coating with chromium gives a shiny, corrosion-resistant finish.
  • Alloying — Mixing metals to change their properties and increase corrosion resistance (e.g., stainless steel).
  • Anodising — Forming a thick oxide layer on aluminium through electrolysis.

Alloys

An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal with a non-metal. Alloys are made to improve properties like hardness, strength, and corrosion resistance.

Did You Know?

Pure gold is 24 carats but too soft for jewellery. So it is alloyed with copper or silver to make 22-carat gold (22 parts gold, 2 parts other metals), which is much harder and more durable. In India, 22-carat gold is the standard for jewellery.
