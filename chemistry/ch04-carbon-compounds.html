Chapter Overview

Understanding why carbon forms the largest number of compounds and the chemistry behind organic molecules.

Why is Carbon Special?

Carbon is one of the most remarkable elements in the periodic table. With atomic number 6 and electronic configuration 2, 4, it has 4 valence electrons. This means carbon can neither easily lose nor gain 4 electrons to attain a noble gas configuration. Instead, it shares electrons to form covalent bonds.

Two unique properties make carbon the king of chemistry:

  • Catenation — Carbon has the unique ability to form bonds with other carbon atoms, creating long chains, branched chains, and rings. This is possible because C–C bonds are very strong (bond energy 348 kJ/mol).
  • Tetravalency — With 4 valence electrons, carbon can form bonds with four other atoms of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulphur, chlorine, and many more elements.

These two properties together allow carbon to form the largest number of compounds known — over 10 million organic compounds have been identified!

Section 2: Bonding in Carbon — Covalent Bond

How atoms share electrons to form stable molecules.

Covalent Bond Formation

A covalent bond is formed by the sharing of electron pairs between two atoms. Each atom contributes one electron to the shared pair. Carbon, with its 4 valence electrons, forms covalent bonds rather than ionic bonds because it would need to gain or lose 4 electrons, which requires too much energy.

Single Bond (One shared pair)

When one pair of electrons is shared between two atoms, a single bond is formed, represented by a single dash (—).

  • H₂ (H–H): Each hydrogen shares 1 electron
  • Cl₂ (Cl–Cl): Each chlorine shares 1 electron
  • CH₄ (methane): Carbon shares 4 electrons with 4 hydrogen atoms

Double Bond (Two shared pairs)

When two pairs of electrons are shared, a double bond (=) is formed.

  • O₂ (O=O): Each oxygen shares 2 electrons
  • C₂H₄ (ethene): Two carbon atoms share 2 electron pairs between them

Triple Bond (Three shared pairs)

When three pairs of electrons are shared, a triple bond (≡) is formed.

  • N₂ (N≡N): Each nitrogen shares 3 electrons
  • C₂H₂ (ethyne): Two carbon atoms share 3 electron pairs

Properties of Covalent Compounds

  • Low melting and boiling points — Weak intermolecular forces (not strong ionic bonds).
  • Poor conductors of electricity — No free ions or electrons to carry charge (exception: graphite).
  • Do not dissociate into ions — Sharing of electrons, not transfer, so no charged particles form.
  • Generally insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents like benzene, acetone.

Allotropes of Carbon

Allotropes are different physical forms of the same element. Carbon has three well-known allotropes:

1. Diamond

  • Each carbon atom is bonded to 4 other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement.
  • Forms a rigid 3D network — hardest natural substance.
  • Does not conduct electricity (no free electrons).
  • Used in cutting tools, jewellery, and drilling.

2. Graphite

  • Each carbon atom is bonded to 3 other carbon atoms in flat hexagonal layers.
  • Layers are held by weak van der Waals forces — layers slide over each other (slippery).
  • The fourth electron is free to move — conducts electricity.
  • Used in pencils, lubricants, and electrodes.

3. Fullerene (C₆₀)

  • Spherical cage of 60 carbon atoms arranged like a soccer ball (12 pentagons, 20 hexagons).
  • Discovered by Harold Kroto, Robert Curl, and Richard Smalley (1985).
  • Used in nanotechnology, drug delivery, and as catalysts.

Fun Fact

Diamond and graphite are both made entirely of carbon atoms, yet diamond is the hardest known natural material while graphite is one of the softest. The difference lies entirely in how the carbon atoms are arranged and bonded!

Section 3: Versatile Nature of Carbon

Chains, branches, rings, and functional groups — the building blocks of organic chemistry.

Saturated and Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes)

Compounds with only single bonds between carbon atoms. General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂

  • Methane: CH₄ (n=1)
  • Ethane: C₂H₆ (n=2)
  • Propane: C₃H₈ (n=3)
  • Butane: C₄H₁₀ (n=4)

Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

Alkenes contain at least one double bond (C=C). General formula: CₙH₂ₙ

Alkynes contain at least one triple bond (C≡C). General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂

Chains, Branches, and Rings

Carbon's ability to catenate allows formation of:

  • Straight chains — Carbon atoms bonded one after another (e.g., butane).
  • Branched chains — Side branches off the main chain (e.g., isobutane).
  • Cyclic/Ring structures — Carbon atoms form closed rings (e.g., cyclohexane C₆H₁₂, benzene C₆H₆).

This structural variety is another reason why carbon forms such a huge number of compounds. Compounds with the same molecular formula but different structures are called structural isomers.

Functional Groups

A functional group is an atom or group of atoms that determines the chemical properties of a compound. Compounds with the same functional group show similar chemical properties.

Alcohol (Hydroxyl) –OH -ol CH₃OH (Methanol)

Homologous Series

A homologous series is a series of compounds with the same general formula, same functional group, and similar chemical properties. Each successive member differs from the previous one by a CH₂ unit (14 u difference in molecular mass).

Properties of a Homologous Series

  • All members have the same general formula.
  • Each member differs from the next by –CH₂– (or 14 u).
  • All members have the same functional group.
  • Physical properties show a gradation — melting point, boiling point, and density increase with molecular mass.
  • Chemical properties are similar due to the same functional group.

Example: Alcohol homologous series — CH₃OH (methanol) → C₂H₅OH (ethanol) → C₃H₇OH (propanol) → C₄H₉OH (butanol).

Section 5: Chemical Properties

Combustion, oxidation, addition, and substitution — the key reactions of carbon compounds.

1. Combustion

Carbon compounds burn in oxygen (air) to produce CO₂, H₂O, and heat and light energy.

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + Energy Methane burns with a clean blue flame
  • Saturated hydrocarbons burn with a clean blue flame (complete combustion).
  • Unsaturated hydrocarbons burn with a sooty yellow flame (incomplete combustion, unburnt carbon particles).
NCERT Tip

Coal and petroleum are major fuels. Saturated hydrocarbons produce more energy per gram and burn more cleanly than unsaturated ones. This is why LPG (mostly saturated) burns with a blue flame.

2. Oxidation

Some carbon compounds can be oxidised using oxidising agents like alkaline KMnO₄ (potassium permanganate) or acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ (potassium dichromate).

CH₃CH₂OH (Ethanol) ⟶ CH₃COOH (Ethanoic acid) Alkaline KMnO₄ or acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ (oxidising agent)

Alcohols are converted to carboxylic acids on oxidation. The substances used for oxidation are called oxidising agents.

3. Addition Reaction

Unsaturated hydrocarbons add hydrogen (H₂) in the presence of catalysts (Ni or Pd) to become saturated. This is called hydrogenation.

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂ ⟶ CH₃–CH₃ Nickel catalyst, 473 K (Ethene → Ethane)

Application: Vegetable oils (unsaturated, liquid) are converted to vegetable ghee/vanaspati (saturated, solid) by hydrogenation. This process is used by the food industry.

4. Substitution Reaction

In saturated hydrocarbons, one or more hydrogen atoms are replaced by another atom or group (like Cl, Br). This only works with saturated compounds.

CH₄ + Cl₂ ⟶ CH₃Cl + HCl In the presence of sunlight (Methane → Chloromethane)

Further substitution can produce CH₂Cl₂ (dichloromethane), CHCl₃ (chloroform/trichloromethane), and CCl₄ (carbon tetrachloride).

Section 6: Important Carbon Compounds

A closer look at ethanol and ethanoic acid — two of the most important carbon compounds.

Ethanol (C₂H₅OH)

Ethanol is the second member of the alcohol homologous series. It is commonly called alcohol or spirit.

Physical Properties

  • Colourless liquid with a pleasant smell.
  • Melting point: 156 K (–117°C); Boiling point: 351 K (78°C).
  • Completely miscible (mixable) with water in all proportions.

Chemical Reactions

Reaction with Sodium:

2C₂H₅OH + 2Na → 2C₂H₅ONa + H₂↑ Sodium ethoxide + Hydrogen gas

Dehydration:

C₂H₅OH ⟶ C₂H₄ + H₂O Conc. H₂SO₄ at 443 K (Ethene is formed)

Conc. H₂SO₄ acts as a dehydrating agent, removing water from ethanol to form ethene (an unsaturated hydrocarbon).

Uses and Harmful Effects

  • Used as a solvent, in medicines (tinctures), alcoholic beverages, and as a fuel.
  • Consumption of methanol (CH₃OH) even in small quantities can cause blindness and death as it is oxidised to methanal (formaldehyde) in the liver.
  • Denatured alcohol: Ethanol mixed with poisonous methanol and dye to prevent its misuse for drinking (used in industries).

Ethanoic Acid (CH₃COOH)

Ethanoic acid belongs to the carboxylic acid group. It is commonly known as acetic acid. Vinegar is a 5–8% solution of acetic acid in water.

Physical Properties

  • Colourless liquid with a pungent, vinegar-like smell.
  • Melting point: 290 K (17°C). Pure acetic acid freezes during winters and looks like ice, hence called glacial acetic acid.
  • Miscible with water.

Chemical Reactions

Esterification (Reaction with alcohol):

CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O Conc. H₂SO₄ catalyst (Ethyl ethanoate/ester + Water)

Esters are sweet-smelling substances used in perfumes and flavouring agents. On treating with NaOH, an ester gives back the alcohol and sodium salt of carboxylic acid. This is called saponification.

Reaction with a base (Neutralisation):

CH₃COOH + NaOH → CH₃COONa + H₂O Sodium ethanoate (salt) + Water

Reaction with carbonates and bicarbonates:

2CH₃COOH + Na₂CO₃ → 2CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂↑ Brisk effervescence of CO₂ gas
CH₃COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂↑

Section 7: Soaps and Detergents

Understanding how soap molecules clean grease — from micelle formation to the hard water problem.

What is Soap?

Soap is a sodium or potassium salt of a long-chain fatty acid (carboxylic acid). Example: Sodium stearate (C₁₇H₃₅COONa).

Saponification

Fat/Oil + NaOH → Soap + Glycerol Saponification reaction (hydrolysis of ester with a base)

Structure of a Soap Molecule

  • Hydrophilic head (ionic end, –COO⁻Na⁺) — water-loving, dissolves in water.
  • Hydrophobic tail (long hydrocarbon chain) — water-repelling, dissolves in oil/grease.

Micelle Formation & Cleaning Action

The animated diagram below shows how soap molecules surround grease to form a micelle and lift it off the surface.

Soaps vs Detergents

  • Soap: Sodium/potassium salt of long-chain carboxylic acid (fatty acid). Does not work well in hard water because it reacts with Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions to form insoluble scum (curdy precipitate).
  • Detergent: Sodium salt of long-chain sulphonic acid or alkyl sulphate. Works effectively in both soft and hard water because its calcium and magnesium salts are soluble in water.
2C₁₇H₃₅COONa + CaCl₂ → (C₁₇H₃₅COO)₂Ca↓ + 2NaCl Soap + Hard water → Insoluble scum (no lather)

Detergents are preferred over soaps for cleaning in hard water areas. However, detergents are non-biodegradable and cause water pollution, while soaps are biodegradable.

Covalent Bonding

  • Single bond: 1 shared pair (e.g., CH₄)
  • Double bond: 2 shared pairs (e.g., C₂H₄)
  • Triple bond: 3 shared pairs (e.g., C₂H₂)
  • Covalent compounds: low MP/BP, poor conductors

Carbon's Versatility

  • Catenation: chains, branches, rings
  • Tetravalency: 4 bonds per carbon atom
  • Allotropes: diamond, graphite, fullerene
  • Homologous series: differ by CH₂

Key Reactions

  • Combustion: produces CO₂ + H₂O + energy
  • Oxidation: alcohol → carboxylic acid
  • Addition: unsaturated → saturated (H₂ + Ni)
  • Substitution: H replaced by Cl in sunlight

Important Compounds

  • Ethanol: BP 351K, reacts with Na, dehydration with H₂SO₄
  • Ethanoic acid: glacial at 290K, esterification, reacts with Na₂CO₃
  • Methanol: poisonous, causes blindness

Soaps & Detergents

  • Soap: Na/K salt of fatty acid, fails in hard water
  • Micelle: hydrophobic tail traps grease, hydrophilic head dissolves in water
  • Detergent: Na salt of sulphonic acid, works in hard water

Nomenclature Rules

  • Root word: meth-, eth-, prop-, but- ... dec-
  • Bond suffix: -ane, -ene, -yne
  • Functional suffix: -ol, -al, -one, -oic acid
  • Always name from longest carbon chain
