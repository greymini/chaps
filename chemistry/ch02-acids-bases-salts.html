Chapter Overview

Understanding the properties, reactions, and applications of acids, bases, and salts.

What Are Acids and Bases?

In everyday life, we encounter acids and bases frequently. Acids taste sour (like lemon juice and vinegar), while bases taste bitter and feel soapy (like soap solution and baking soda). However, tasting chemicals in the laboratory is dangerous and never recommended.

Instead, we use special substances called indicators to identify acids and bases. An indicator changes colour in acidic and basic solutions. Common indicators include litmus, methyl orange, and phenolphthalein.

Chemically, acids are substances that produce hydrogen ions (H⁺) or hydronium ions (H₃O⁺) in water, while bases are substances that produce hydroxide ions (OH⁻) in water.

Chemical Properties of Acids & Bases

Key reactions that define how acids and bases behave with different substances.

Reaction of Acids with Metals

Acids react with metals to form a salt and release hydrogen gas (H₂). The hydrogen gas produced can be tested by bringing a burning candle near it — it burns with a ‘pop’ sound.

Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen Gas (H₂ ↑)
2HCl + Zn → ZnCl₂ + H₂ ↑ Hydrochloric acid + Zinc → Zinc chloride + Hydrogen gas
2HNO₃ + Zn → Zn(NO₃)₂ + H₂ ↑ Dilute nitric acid + Zinc → Zinc nitrate + Hydrogen gas

Note: Bases generally do not react with metals. However, strong bases like NaOH react with reactive metals like zinc and aluminium to produce hydrogen gas.

2NaOH + Zn → Na₂ZnO₂ + H₂ ↑ Sodium hydroxide + Zinc → Sodium zincate + Hydrogen gas

Reaction with Metal Carbonates & Bicarbonates

When acids react with metal carbonates or metal bicarbonates (hydrogen carbonates), the products are a salt, water, and carbon dioxide gas (CO₂). The CO₂ turns lime water milky — a classic test for the gas.

Metal Carbonate + Acid → Salt + H₂O + CO₂ ↑
Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ ↑ Sodium carbonate + Hydrochloric acid → Sodium chloride + Water + Carbon dioxide
NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ ↑ Sodium bicarbonate + HCl → Sodium chloride + Water + Carbon dioxide
Lime Water Test

When CO₂ is passed through lime water [Ca(OH)₂], it turns milky due to the formation of insoluble calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). On passing excess CO₂, the milkiness disappears as soluble calcium bicarbonate [Ca(HCO₃)₂] is formed.

Neutralization Reaction

When an acid reacts with a base, they neutralize each other to form a salt and water. This is called a neutralization reaction.

Acid + Base → Salt + Water
NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O Sodium hydroxide + Hydrochloric acid → Sodium chloride + Water

Reactions of Metallic & Non-metallic Oxides

Metallic Oxides + Acids

Metallic oxides react with acids to produce a salt and water, similar to a base. This shows that metallic oxides are basic in nature.

CuO + 2HCl → CuCl₂ + H₂O Copper oxide + Hydrochloric acid → Copper chloride + Water (Blue-green solution)

Non-metallic Oxides + Bases

Non-metallic oxides react with bases to produce a salt and water, similar to an acid. This shows that non-metallic oxides are acidic in nature.

CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O Carbon dioxide + Calcium hydroxide → Calcium carbonate + Water (Lime water turns milky)

Role of Water in Acid-Base Chemistry

Acids produce H⁺ (hydrogen ions) or H₃O⁺ (hydronium ions) only in the presence of water. Dry acids do not show acidic behaviour. For example, dry HCl gas does not change the colour of litmus paper, but HCl dissolved in water (hydrochloric acid) turns blue litmus red.

HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻ Hydrochloric acid produces hydronium ions in water
NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻ Sodium hydroxide dissociates to produce hydroxide ions in water
Safety Rule: Dilution

Dissolving an acid or base in water is highly exothermic (releases a lot of heat). Always add acid to water slowly, never water to acid! Adding water to concentrated acid can cause the mixture to splash out due to the intense heat generated, causing severe burns. The recommended process is to make a thin stream of acid while stirring the water constantly.

Olfactory Indicators

Some substances change their smell when mixed with an acid or a base. These are called olfactory indicators.

  • Onion: Has a characteristic strong smell. The smell disappears in a basic solution but remains in an acidic solution.
  • Vanilla extract: Its distinct pleasant smell disappears in a basic solution.
  • Clove oil: Loses its smell in a basic solution.

Olfactory indicators are useful for visually impaired students who cannot see colour changes in litmus or other indicators.

The pH Scale

A universal measure of how acidic or basic a solution is.

Understanding pH

The pH scale ranges from 0 to 14 and measures the concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺) in a solution. The term pH stands for “potential of hydrogen”.

  • pH < 7 — Acidic solution (more H⁺ ions)
  • pH = 7 — Neutral (pure water)
  • pH > 7 — Basic/alkaline solution (more OH⁻ ions)

A universal indicator is a mixture of several indicators that shows different colours at different pH values, allowing us to determine the exact pH of a solution.

Strong acids (like HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃) have low pH values (close to 0), while weak acids (like acetic acid, citric acid) have pH values closer to 7 but still below it. Similarly, strong bases (NaOH, KOH) have high pH values (close to 14), while weak bases (NH₄OH) have pH closer to 7 but above it.

pH in Everyday Life

1. Blood pH (7.35 – 7.45)

Our body maintains blood pH in a very narrow range of 7.35 to 7.45. Any significant change in this pH can be life-threatening. The body uses buffer systems to maintain this precise balance.

2. Stomach pH (~1.2)

The stomach secretes hydrochloric acid (HCl) which helps in digestion. When excess acid is produced (acidity), we take antacids (mild bases like milk of magnesia) to neutralize it.

3. Tooth Decay (below pH 5.5)

Bacteria in the mouth produce acids by degrading sugar and food particles. When the pH in the mouth falls below 5.5, the tooth enamel (calcium phosphate, the hardest substance in the body) begins to corrode. Using toothpaste (basic) helps neutralize the acid and prevent decay.

4. Soil pH & Agriculture

Most plants grow best in soil with a pH between 6 and 7. If soil is too acidic, farmers add quicklime (CaO) or slaked lime (Ca(OH)₂) to neutralize it. If too basic, organic matter or gypsum is added.

5. Bee Stings & Wasp Stings

A bee sting injects formic acid (methanoic acid) — apply baking soda (a mild base) to neutralize it. A wasp sting is alkaline — apply vinegar (a mild acid) to neutralize it.

6. Self-Defence by Plants & Animals

Nettle plant stings inject methanoic acid. The traditional remedy is rubbing with dock leaves (which contain a base) to neutralize the acid and relieve the sting.

Fun Fact

Your stomach acid (HCl) has a pH of about 1.2 — strong enough to dissolve metal! But the mucus lining of your stomach protects it from being digested by its own acid. This mucus lining is replaced every 3-4 days to maintain protection.

Important Salts & Their Uses

From common salt to plaster of Paris — the chemistry behind everyday materials.

Common Salt (NaCl) & Chlor-Alkali Process

Sodium chloride (NaCl) — common salt — is obtained from sea water, rock salt deposits, or salt lakes. It is the raw material for producing many important chemicals.

The Chlor-Alkali Process

When electricity is passed through an aqueous solution of sodium chloride (brine), it decomposes to form sodium hydroxide, chlorine gas, and hydrogen gas. This is called the chlor-alkali process because it produces chlor (chlorine) and alkali (NaOH).

2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g) + H₂(g) Electrolysis of brine (chlor-alkali process)

Uses of the products:

  • Chlorine (Cl₂) — water purification, disinfectants, PVC pipes, CFC compounds, bleaching.
  • Hydrogen (H₂) — fuels, manufacturing ammonia (Haber process), hydrogenating vegetable oils to make margarine.
  • Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) — degreasing metals, manufacturing soaps, detergents, paper, and artificial fibres.

Bleaching Powder (CaOCl₂)

Bleaching powder is produced by the action of chlorine gas on dry slaked lime [Ca(OH)₂].

Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O Slaked lime + Chlorine → Bleaching powder + Water

Chemical name: Calcium oxychloride (CaOCl₂)

Uses:

  • Bleaching cotton, linen, and wood pulp in textile and paper industries.
  • Disinfecting drinking water to make it germ-free.
  • As an oxidising agent in many chemical industries.

Baking Soda (NaHCO₃)

Chemical name: Sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO₃), also called sodium bicarbonate.

NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ + NH₃ → NH₄Cl + NaHCO₃ Solvay process (industrial preparation)

Uses:

  • Baking: On heating, NaHCO₃ releases CO₂ which causes bread/cake to rise and become soft and fluffy.
  • Antacid: Being mildly basic, it neutralizes excess stomach acid and relieves indigestion.
  • Fire extinguisher: Soda-acid fire extinguishers use NaHCO₃ with acid to generate CO₂ that smothers fire.
  • Baking powder is a mixture of baking soda (NaHCO₃) and a mild edible acid like tartaric acid. When baking powder is heated or mixed with water, tartaric acid neutralizes Na₂CO₃ formed, preventing a bitter taste.
2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ ↑ On heating, baking soda decomposes (used in baking)

Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O)

Chemical name: Sodium carbonate decahydrate.

Washing soda is obtained by heating baking soda and then recrystallising with water:

2NaHCO₃ ⟶ Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ Step 1: Heating baking soda gives anhydrous sodium carbonate
Na₂CO₃ + 10H₂O → Na₂CO₃·10H₂O Step 2: Recrystallisation gives washing soda

Uses:

  • Removing permanent hardness of water (softening water).
  • Manufacturing glass, soap, and paper.
  • Used as a cleaning agent in domestic and industrial settings.
  • Manufacturing sodium compounds like borax.

Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O)

Chemical name: Calcium sulphate hemihydrate.

Plaster of Paris is obtained by heating gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) at 373 K (100°C).

CaSO₄·2H₂O ⟶ CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O Gypsum → Plaster of Paris + Water (on heating at 373 K)

Setting reaction: When plaster of Paris is mixed with water, it sets into a hard solid mass (gypsum) again. This property makes it extremely useful.

CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O Plaster of Paris + Water → Gypsum (hard solid) — setting reaction

Uses:

  • Making casts for setting fractured bones in hospitals.
  • Making toys, decorative materials, and statues.
  • Creating smooth surfaces (wall plastering).
  • Making moulds in dentistry.
Important Note

If gypsum is heated above 373 K (above 100°C), all the water of crystallisation is removed, forming anhydrous calcium sulphate (dead burnt plaster), which cannot be set by adding water. Temperature control is crucial during preparation.

pH Scale Summary

pH 0-2: Strong acids (HCl, H₂SO₄)
pH 3-4: Weak acids (vinegar, lemon juice)
pH 5-6: Mildly acidic (coffee, rain water)
pH 7: Neutral (pure water)
pH 8-9: Mildly basic (baking soda, sea water)
pH 10-12: Moderately basic (soap, milk of magnesia)
pH 13-14: Strong bases (NaOH, KOH)

Acids

  • Produce H⁺/H₃O⁺ ions in water.
  • Turn blue litmus red.
  • React with metals to give H₂.
  • React with carbonates to give CO₂.

Bases

  • Produce OH⁻ ions in water.
  • Turn red litmus blue.
  • Bitter taste, soapy feel.
  • React with non-metallic oxides.

Indicators

  • Litmus: red in acid, blue in base.
  • Methyl orange: red/pink in acid, yellow in base.
  • Phenolphthalein: colourless in acid, pink in base.
  • Universal indicator shows full pH range.

Important Salts

  • NaCl: chlor-alkali process.
  • NaHCO₃: baking, antacid, fire extinguisher.
  • Na₂CO₃: cleaning, softening water.
  • CaOCl₂: bleaching, disinfection.
  • CaSO₄·½H₂O: casts, moulds, plastering.

pH in Daily Life

  • Blood: 7.35-7.45 (tightly regulated).
  • Stomach: ~1.2 (HCl for digestion).
  • Tooth decay: below pH 5.5.
  • Bee sting: formic acid (use baking soda).

Safety Rules

  • Always add acid to water, not water to acid.
  • Dilution is exothermic (releases heat).
  • Never taste chemicals in the lab.
  • Use indicators, not taste or touch.
