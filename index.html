Before you can see anything, light must travel, bounce, and bend. Here's how it does all three.

Reflection of Light

The Laws of Bouncing Light

When you look in a mirror, you're witnessing one of physics' most elegant rules in action. The moment light strikes a polished surface, it obeys two laws without exception — laws so consistent that we can use them to engineer telescopes, headlights, and solar furnaces.

⚡ The Two Laws of Reflection

Law 1: The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection — always. ∠i = ∠r
Law 2: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of contact all lie in the same plane.

The plane mirror you use every morning forms an image that is virtual, erect, same-sized, and placed exactly as far behind the mirror as you are in front of it. That slightly unreal quality — where you can never actually reach through and touch the image — is exactly what "virtual" means in optics.

⚡
Did You Know?

Light travels at 3 × 10⁸ m/s — fast enough to circle the entire Earth 7.5 times in a single second. Yet the moment it enters glass or water, it slows down — and that slowdown is exactly what makes lenses and prisms work.

✦
Spherical Mirrors

Curved Mirrors — Where Things Get Interesting

Bend the mirror's surface into a sphere and the physics transforms dramatically. A concave mirror — curved inward like a satellite dish — converges parallel rays of light to a single point. A convex mirror — curved outward like the back of a spoon — spreads them apart.

Mirror Formula
1/v + 1/u = 1/f
v = image distance · u = object distance · f = focal length
Magnification
m = −v / u
Negative m → real & inverted. Positive m → virtual & erect.
Focal Length Relation
f = R / 2
R = radius of curvature. Focal length is always half the radius.
Sign Convention
Left ← P → Right
Distances to the right (+), to the left (−), upward (+), downward (−).

💡 Why Convex Mirrors in Rear-View?

A convex mirror always produces a virtual, erect, diminished image — regardless of where the object is. That diminishment is actually a feature: a smaller image means a wider scene, giving the driver a larger field of view behind the car. The trade-off? Distances appear farther than they really are — hence the warning printed on every rear-view mirror.

Worked Examples

Practice Makes Perfect

1
A convex mirror has radius of curvature 3 m. A bus is 5 m away. Find the position, nature, and size of the image.
▾
1

Identify given values and apply sign convention.

R = +3.00 m → f = R/2 = +1.50 m  |  u = −5.00 m
2

Apply the mirror formula to find image distance.

1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/1.50 + 1/5.00 = 0.667 + 0.200 = 0.867
v = +1.15 m
3

Calculate magnification.

m = −v/u = −1.15 / (−5.00) = +0.23
✓ Answer

Image forms 1.15 m behind the mirror — virtual, erect, and diminished to 0.23× the size of the bus. Positive v confirms it's behind the mirror; positive m confirms erect & virtual.

2
A 4 cm object is placed 25 cm in front of a concave mirror (f = 15 cm). Where must a screen be placed for a sharp image?
▾
1

Sign convention for concave mirror: f is negative.

h = +4.0 cm  |  u = −25 cm  |  f = −15 cm
2

Apply mirror formula.

1/v = 1/f − 1/u = −1/15 + 1/25 = (−5+3)/75 = −2/75
v = −37.5 cm
3

Find magnification and image height.

m = −v/u = −37.5/(−25) = −1.5  |  h′ = −1.5 × 4 = −6.0 cm
✓ Answer

Screen placed 37.5 cm in front of the mirror. Image is real, inverted, and 6 cm tall — 1.5× enlarged. The negative m confirms it is real and inverted.

Refraction of Light

When Light Changes Its Path

Light travels at a staggering 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum. Drop it into water, and it slows to about 2.25 × 10⁸ m/s. That change in speed, at an angle, is exactly what causes light to bend — and it's why a straw looks broken in a glass of water.

📐 Snell's Law

sin i / sin r = n₂₁ = v₁/v₂
The ratio of the sines of the angles equals the ratio of speeds in the two media — and the refractive index of the second medium relative to the first.

The Rules of Bending

The direction of bending follows one simple rule: light bends toward the normal when entering a denser medium, and away from the normal when entering a rarer one. Dense = slower speed = higher refractive index.

Refractive Index of Common Materials

MaterialRefractive Index (n)Speed of Light
Air≈ 1.00≈ 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s
Water1.332.26 × 10⁸ m/s
Crown Glass1.521.97 × 10⁸ m/s
Diamond2.421.24 × 10⁸ m/s

Diamond's refractive index of 2.42 means light travels 2.42× slower inside it than in air — which is why diamonds sparkle so spectacularly.

💎
Real World Connection

The total internal reflection of light in optical fibre cables is the same Snell's Law principle — light trapped inside glass fibres carries your internet data across continents at nearly the speed of light.

✦
Lenses

Lenses — Controlled Refraction

A lens is essentially a carefully shaped block of glass that uses refraction at two surfaces to bend light exactly where you want it. Convex lenses converge light to a focus; concave lenses diverge it as if it came from a focus.

Lens Formula
1/v − 1/u = 1/f
Note the minus sign — unlike the mirror formula!
Power of Lens
P = 1/f (metres)
Unit: Dioptre (D). Convex = +P, Concave = −P.
Magnification
m = v / u
Note: no negative sign, unlike mirrors.
Combination
P = P₁ + P₂ + …
Powers add algebraically for lenses in contact.

Chapter 9 — Key Takeaways

Reflection laws: ∠i = ∠r, all rays in the same plane.

Concave mirror: converges light; used in torches, solar furnaces, dentist mirrors.

Convex mirror: always virtual, erect, diminished — rear-view mirrors.

Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f  |  R = 2f

Refraction: light bends at media interfaces; Snell's Law governs the angle.

Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f  |  P = 1/f (dioptres)

The Human Eye &
The Colourful World

You have the most sophisticated optical instrument ever built sitting in your skull. Here's how it works — and why the world looks the way it does.

The Human Eye

Nature's Finest Lens System

Your eye is a camera that can autofocus in milliseconds, adjust its aperture across a 100,000:1 range of brightness, and has never needed a firmware update in its 500-million-year history. At its core is a beautiful lens system that works by the same physics you just studied.

🧬 How the Eye Works

Light enters through the cornea (does most of the bending), passes through the pupil (aperture controlled by the iris), and is fine-focused by the crystalline lens — whose shape is actively controlled by ciliary muscles — onto the retina, which sends electrical signals to the brain via the optic nerve.

Power of Accommodation

The eye's killer feature: accommodation. Unlike a camera lens that moves back and forth to focus, your eye lens changes its own shape. Ciliary muscles contract to make the lens thicker (more powerful) for near objects, and relax to make it thinner (less powerful) for distant ones.

👁️
Mind-Blowing Fact

Your eye can distinguish roughly 10 million different colours. The retina has ~120 million rod cells for low-light vision and ~6 million cone cells for colour — and the entire image-processing happens in real time, continuously, without any effort from you.

Near Point (Normal)
25 cm
Closest distance for comfortable clear vision. Also called least distance of distinct vision.
Far Point (Normal)
∞ (Infinity)
Farthest distance the eye can see clearly without strain.
✦
Defects of Vision

When the Eye Gets It Wrong

Three things can go wrong with an eye: the eyeball can be too long or too short, the lens can be too curved or too flat, or the ciliary muscles can weaken with age. Each creates a characteristic defect — and each has an elegant optical fix.

🔍
Myopia
Near-sightedness

The eyeball is too long, so parallel rays from distant objects converge before the retina. Near objects are clear; far objects are blurry. Far point is closer than infinity.

🔵 Corrected with a concave (diverging) lens — it pushes the focal point back onto the retina.
🔭
Hypermetropia
Far-sightedness

The eyeball is too short, so light from nearby objects would converge behind the retina. Distant objects are clear; close objects are blurry. Near point is beyond 25 cm.

🔵 Corrected with a convex (converging) lens — it brings the focus forward onto the retina.
👴 Presbyopia — The Age Defect

As we age, the ciliary muscles gradually weaken and the crystalline lens loses flexibility. The result: the eye can no longer accommodate as well. Reading glasses (for near) and distance glasses (for far) are both needed — often solved with bifocal lenses that have two different powers in a single glass.

Dispersion & Scattering

Why the World Is So Colourful

White sunlight is not truly "white" — it's a superposition of every colour from violet to red. A glass prism splits it apart by bending each wavelength a different amount. The result is the spectrum that Newton first catalogued: VIBGYOR.

Violet (380nm) Indigo Blue Green Yellow Orange Red (700nm)

Violet has the shortest wavelength and bends the most. Red has the longest wavelength and bends the least.

✦
Natural Phenomena

The Physics Behind Everyday Wonders

Every natural spectacle in the sky — the blue dome above, the orange disc at sunset, the twinkling of stars, the arc of a rainbow — is optics in action. Here's the science behind four of the most beautiful ones.

🌤️
Blue Sky
Air molecules scatter shorter wavelengths (blue ≈ 450 nm) far more than longer ones. Blue light is scattered ~10× more than red. When you look anywhere in the sky (away from the sun), you're seeing this scattered blue light — hence the blue dome.
🌅
Red Sunrise & Sunset
At the horizon, sunlight travels through a much thicker slice of atmosphere. By the time it reaches you, nearly all blue has scattered away. Only the least-scattered red and orange survive the journey — painting the sky in warm hues.
🌈
Rainbow
Rain droplets act as tiny prisms. Light enters a droplet, undergoes dispersion + total internal reflection + dispersion again as it exits. Different colours exit at slightly different angles, separating into an arc. Always appears opposite to the sun.
✨
Twinkling Stars
Stars are so distant they appear as point sources. Their light passes through layers of atmosphere with constantly shifting density — causing continuous refraction that makes the apparent position and brightness flicker. Planets don't twinkle because they appear as tiny discs, not points.

Atmospheric Refraction

How the Atmosphere Bends the World

The atmosphere isn't a uniform slab of glass — it's a gradient, thickening and densening toward the Earth. Light passing through this gradient bends continuously, creating effects that have mystified humans for centuries.

⏰ Advance Sunrise & Delayed Sunset

Because the atmosphere bends the sun's light, we see the sun about 2 minutes before it actually crosses the horizon at sunrise — and for 2 minutes after it's geometrically below the horizon at sunset. The day is literally 4 minutes longer than it would be in a universe without atmosphere. That slight flattening you see in the setting sun is also atmospheric refraction at work.

Chapter 10 — Key Takeaways

Near point = 25 cm; Far point = ∞ for a normal eye.

Accommodation: ciliary muscles change lens curvature to focus at different distances.

Myopia → concave lens. Hypermetropia → convex lens. Presbyopia → bifocals.

Dispersion: white light splits into VIBGYOR; violet bends most, red bends least.

Blue sky: Rayleigh scattering — blue (~450nm) scattered ~10× more than red.

Twinkling: point-source stars vs disc-source planets — only points twinkle.
