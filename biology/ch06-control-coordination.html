Topic 1

Nervous System & Neurons

The structural and functional unit of the nervous system is the neuron — a specialised cell that transmits nerve impulses.

Structure of a Neuron

A neuron consists of three main parts:

  • Dendrites: Short, branching projections that receive information from other neurons or receptors and carry impulses towards the cell body.
  • Cell Body (Soma): Contains the nucleus and cytoplasm. It processes incoming signals and maintains the cell.
  • Axon: A long, slender projection that carries impulses away from the cell body towards the axon terminals. Often covered by a myelin sheath (insulating fatty layer) that speeds up impulse transmission.
  • Axon Terminal (Synaptic Knob): The branched endings of the axon that form synapses with the next neuron. They release neurotransmitters to transmit the signal across the gap.

Types of Neurons

  • Sensory Neurons (Afferent): Carry impulses from receptors (sense organs) to the CNS (brain or spinal cord).
  • Motor Neurons (Efferent): Carry impulses from the CNS to effectors (muscles or glands).
  • Relay Neurons (Interneurons): Found within the CNS; connect sensory and motor neurons. They process and relay information.

Nerve Impulse & Synapse

A nerve impulse is an electrochemical signal that travels along a neuron. At rest, a neuron has a negative charge inside (resting potential). When stimulated, ions flow across the membrane causing depolarisation, which propagates the impulse along the axon.

At the synapse (the tiny gap between two neurons), the electrical signal triggers the release of chemical messengers called neurotransmitters (e.g., acetylcholine). These cross the synaptic cleft and bind to receptors on the next neuron, generating a new electrical impulse. This ensures impulses travel in one direction only.

Topic 2

Human Nervous System

The human nervous system is divided into the Central Nervous System (CNS) and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS).

Central Nervous System (CNS)

The CNS consists of the brain and the spinal cord. It is the main processing centre of the body.

Regions of the Brain

  • Cerebrum: The largest part. Controls voluntary actions, thinking, memory, reasoning, and sensory processing. Divided into two hemispheres.
  • Cerebellum: Located at the back of the brain. Controls balance, posture, and coordination of voluntary movements.
  • Medulla Oblongata: Connects the brain to the spinal cord. Controls involuntary actions such as breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, and swallowing.

Spinal Cord: A long, cylindrical structure running through the vertebral column. It transmits impulses between the brain and the rest of the body, and is the centre for reflex actions.

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

The PNS includes all the nerves that branch out from the brain and spinal cord to the rest of the body. It has two divisions:

  • Voluntary (Somatic) Nervous System: Controls actions we consciously decide to do — walking, writing, speaking.
  • Involuntary (Autonomic) Nervous System: Controls actions that happen without conscious thought — digestion, heart rate, pupil dilation. It has two branches: sympathetic (fight or flight) and parasympathetic (rest and digest).

Topic 3

Reflex Actions & Reflex Arc

A reflex action is a spontaneous, involuntary, rapid response to a stimulus that does not involve conscious thought.

What is a Reflex Action?

Reflex actions are automatic, rapid responses to stimuli that protect us from harm. They are controlled by the spinal cord, not the brain, which is why they happen so quickly. The brain is only informed after the action has already occurred.

Examples:

  • Pulling your hand away from a hot object
  • Knee-jerk reflex when tapped below the kneecap
  • Blinking when something comes close to your eye
  • Coughing or sneezing

The Reflex Arc

The pathway taken by a nerve impulse during a reflex action is called a reflex arc:

Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory Neuron → Spinal Cord (Relay) → Motor Neuron → Effector → Response

Topic 4

Plant Movements (Tropisms)

Plants do not have a nervous system, but they respond to stimuli using plant hormones — primarily auxin.

Tropic Movements (Tropisms)

Tropisms are directional growth responses of a plant towards or away from a stimulus. Growth towards the stimulus is positive tropism; growth away is negative tropism.

  • Phototropism: Growth response to light. Shoots show positive phototropism (grow towards light). Auxin accumulates on the darker side, causing more elongation there, bending the shoot toward light.
  • Geotropism (Gravitropism): Growth response to gravity. Roots show positive geotropism (grow downward); shoots show negative geotropism (grow upward).
  • Hydrotropism: Growth of roots towards water.
  • Thigmotropism: Growth response to touch. Example: tendrils of pea plants coil around a support.

Nastic Movements vs Tropic Movements

Nastic movements are non-directional responses to stimuli — the direction of the stimulus does not determine the direction of the response. They happen due to changes in water pressure (turgor) in cells.

  • Example 1: The "touch-me-not" plant (Mimosa pudica) folds its leaves when touched — regardless of which direction the touch comes from.
  • Example 2: Opening and closing of flowers in response to light/dark.

Key difference: Tropic movements are directional and involve growth; nastic movements are non-directional and involve changes in turgor pressure (not growth).

Topic 5

Chemical Coordination — Hormones

Hormones are chemical messengers secreted by endocrine glands that travel via the blood to target organs and control various body functions.

Human Endocrine System

Endocrine glands are ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream. Key glands:

Key Human Hormones

  • Growth Hormone (GH): Secreted by the pituitary gland. Controls growth and development of the body. Deficiency in childhood causes dwarfism; excess causes gigantism.
  • Thyroxine: Secreted by the thyroid gland. Regulates metabolism of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. Requires iodine for synthesis. Deficiency causes goitre (swollen neck).
  • Insulin: Secreted by the pancreas (beta cells of Islets of Langerhans). Lowers blood sugar by promoting glucose absorption by cells. Deficiency causes diabetes mellitus.
  • Adrenaline: Secreted by the adrenal glands. Known as the "fight or flight" hormone. Increases heart rate, breathing rate, and blood flow to muscles during stress.
  • Testosterone: Secreted by the testes. Controls development of male secondary sexual characteristics (facial hair, deep voice, muscle growth).
  • Oestrogen: Secreted by the ovaries. Controls development of female secondary sexual characteristics (breast development, regulation of menstrual cycle).

Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)

Plants use chemical substances to coordinate growth and responses to the environment:

  • Auxin: Promotes cell elongation. Responsible for phototropism — moves to the shaded side, causing cells there to elongate more, bending the plant towards light. Also involved in root initiation.
  • Gibberellin: Promotes stem elongation and seed germination. Helps break seed dormancy and stimulates flowering.
  • Cytokinin: Promotes cell division (cytokinesis). Present mainly in areas of rapid cell division like fruits and seeds. Delays leaf ageing.
  • Abscisic Acid (ABA): Inhibits growth. Known as the "stress hormone" of plants. Causes stomata to close during water stress (wilting). Promotes seed dormancy and leaf fall.
  • Ethylene: A gaseous hormone. Promotes fruit ripening, leaf fall (abscission), and flower wilting. Used commercially to ripen fruits.

Recap

Key Takeaways

  • Neurons are the structural and functional units of the nervous system. They transmit electrochemical impulses via dendrites, cell body, and axon.
  • The synapse is the tiny gap between neurons where neurotransmitters carry the signal chemically from one neuron to the next.
  • The CNS (brain + spinal cord) is the main processing centre. The PNS connects the CNS to the rest of the body.
  • The cerebrum handles thinking, cerebellum handles balance, and medulla oblongata handles involuntary actions like breathing.
  • Reflex actions are rapid, involuntary responses mediated by the spinal cord via a reflex arc.
  • Plants respond to stimuli through tropisms (directional growth) controlled by hormones like auxin.
  • Phototropism works because auxin moves to the darker side, causing more cell elongation there — bending the shoot toward light.
  • Nastic movements (e.g., Mimosa) are non-directional and caused by changes in turgor pressure, not growth.
  • The endocrine system uses ductless glands to secrete hormones into the blood for slow, long-term coordination.
  • Key hormones: insulin (blood sugar), adrenaline (fight or flight), thyroxine (metabolism), growth hormone (development), testosterone/oestrogen (sexual characteristics).
  • Plant hormones: auxin (elongation), gibberellin (growth), cytokinin (cell division), ABA (stress/inhibition), ethylene (ripening).
