Topic 1

Heredity and Variation

What is Heredity?

Heredity is the process by which traits and characteristics are transmitted from parents to their offspring through genes. It is the reason children resemble their parents. Genes, located on chromosomes, carry the information for specific traits like eye colour, height, and blood group.

Every organism inherits one set of genes from each parent. The combination of these genes determines the organism's characteristics. This is why offspring are similar to, but not identical copies of, their parents.

What is Variation?

Variation refers to the differences in characteristics among individuals of the same species. Even though children inherit genes from the same parents, siblings are not identical to each other (except identical twins). This is because sexual reproduction creates new combinations of genes during meiosis and fertilisation.

Importance of Variation:

  • Variation is the raw material for evolution.
  • It ensures that a species can adapt to changing environments.
  • If a population has no variation, a single environmental change could wipe out the entire species.
  • For example, if all bacteria in a population are identical and an antibiotic is introduced, all would die. But if some have a variation making them resistant, those survive and reproduce.

Sources of Variation

Rules of Inheritance

Key terms to understand before we study Mendel's work:

  • Gene: A unit of inheritance. It is a segment of DNA on a chromosome that codes for a specific protein, which determines a particular trait.
  • Allele: Different forms of the same gene. For example, the gene for height has two alleles — T (tall) and t (short).
  • Genotype: The genetic makeup of an organism (e.g., TT, Tt, tt).
  • Phenotype: The physical appearance of an organism based on its genotype (e.g., tall or short).
  • Homozygous: Both alleles are the same (TT or tt).
  • Heterozygous: The two alleles are different (Tt).
  • Dominant trait: Expresses itself in both homozygous (TT) and heterozygous (Tt) condition. Written as a capital letter.
  • Recessive trait: Expresses itself only in homozygous condition (tt). Written as a lowercase letter.
Fun Fact: Even identical twins, who share 100% of their DNA, can develop slight differences due to environmental factors. These are called epigenetic changes!

Topic 2

Mendel's Experiments

Gregor Johann Mendel (1822–1884)

Gregor Mendel, an Austrian monk, is known as the "Father of Genetics". He performed systematic breeding experiments on garden pea plants (Pisum sativum) for 7 years and studied the inheritance of 7 pairs of contrasting traits: seed shape, seed colour, flower colour, pod shape, pod colour, flower position, and plant height.

He chose pea plants because they have short generation times, produce many offspring, have clearly contrasting traits, and can self-pollinate or be cross-pollinated easily.

Mendel's Seven Contrasting Traits in Pea Plants

Monohybrid Cross

A monohybrid cross studies the inheritance of one pair of contrasting characters.

Example: Tall (TT) × Short (tt)

  • F1 Generation: All plants are Tall (Tt) — the tall trait is dominant, short is recessive.
  • F2 Generation: When F1 plants (Tt × Tt) are self-pollinated, the ratio is 3 Tall : 1 Short.
  • Genotypic ratio: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt

Monohybrid Cross: Tt × Tt

Dihybrid Cross

A dihybrid cross studies the inheritance of two pairs of contrasting characters simultaneously.

Example: Round Yellow (RRYY) × Wrinkled Green (rryy)

  • F1 Generation: All plants are Round Yellow (RrYy).
  • F2 Generation: 9 Round Yellow : 3 Round Green : 3 Wrinkled Yellow : 1 Wrinkled Green

Dihybrid Cross F2 Ratio — 9:3:3:1

Mendel's Laws of Inheritance

1. Law of Dominance: When two organisms with contrasting traits are crossed, only the dominant trait appears in the F1 generation. The recessive trait is masked but not lost.

2. Law of Segregation (Law of Purity of Gametes): Each individual possesses two alleles for each trait. During gamete formation, these alleles segregate (separate) so that each gamete carries only one allele for each trait.

3. Law of Independent Assortment: When two or more pairs of traits are considered together, the alleles of different traits assort independently of one another during gamete formation. This is why the dihybrid cross gives a 9:3:3:1 ratio rather than a 3:1 ratio.

Topic 3

Sex Determination

XX/XY System in Humans

In humans, sex is determined by sex chromosomes. Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes — 22 pairs are autosomes (body chromosomes) and 1 pair are sex chromosomes.

  • Females have two X chromosomes: XX
  • Males have one X and one Y chromosome: XY

All eggs (ova) from the mother carry an X chromosome. Sperm from the father carry either an X or a Y chromosome. Therefore, the father's sperm determines the sex of the child:

  • If X sperm fertilises the egg → XX → Girl
  • If Y sperm fertilises the egg → XY → Boy

Sex Determination in Humans

Fun Fact: In some species like birds and butterflies, the system is reversed: males are ZZ and females are ZW! In some reptiles like crocodiles, the sex is determined by the temperature at which eggs are incubated.

Topic 4

Evolution

Accumulation of Variation

Evolution is the gradual change in the inherited characteristics of a population over successive generations. It occurs due to the accumulation of variations over many generations.

In asexual reproduction, variations arise only through mutations, so evolution is slow. In sexual reproduction, new combinations of genes are created in every generation due to crossing over and random assortment. This creates much more variation and accelerates evolution.

Natural Selection (Darwin)

Charles Darwin proposed the theory of natural selection (also called "survival of the fittest") based on his observations during a voyage on HMS Beagle.

Key points:

  • Organisms produce more offspring than can survive.
  • There is a struggle for existence due to limited resources.
  • Individuals with favourable variations are better adapted and survive more often.
  • These survivors reproduce and pass their advantageous traits to the next generation.
  • Over many generations, this leads to the evolution of new characteristics and even new species.

Natural Selection in Action

Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species from existing ones. It occurs when populations become reproductively isolated and accumulate enough genetic differences over time.

Key mechanisms:

  • Geographical isolation: A physical barrier (mountain, river) splits a population. Each group evolves independently due to different environments and random genetic changes.
  • Genetic drift: Random changes in allele frequency in small, isolated populations. Chance events can dramatically alter which genes are common, especially after a population bottleneck.
  • Natural selection: Different environments favour different traits, driving the two groups further apart genetically.

Over time, these isolated groups accumulate so many differences that they can no longer interbreed even if the barrier is removed — they have become separate species.

Speciation through Geographical Isolation

Acquired Traits vs Inherited Traits

Acquired traits are changes in an organism that develop during its lifetime due to environmental factors or personal actions. They affect only the somatic (body) cells, not the reproductive cells, so they are NOT passed on to the next generation.

Examples: A bodybuilder's large muscles, a surgeon's skill, a plant losing leaves due to drought, scars.

Inherited traits are traits coded by genes in the DNA. They are present in the germ cells (reproductive cells) and are passed on to offspring.

Examples: Eye colour, blood group, attached/free earlobes, hair texture.

Key difference: Only changes in the DNA of germ cells (gametes) can be inherited. Changes in body cells, no matter how significant, die with the organism. This is why Lamarck's theory of inheritance of acquired traits was rejected.

Fun Fact: Darwin's finches on the Galapagos Islands had different beak shapes depending on the food available on each island. This was a key observation that led Darwin to formulate his theory of natural selection!

Topic 5

Tracing Evolutionary Relationships

Homologous Organs

Homologous organs have the same basic structural plan and origin but perform different functions. They indicate divergent evolution from a common ancestor.

Example: The forelimbs of a human (grasping), a bat (flying), a whale (swimming), and a horse (running) all have the same bone structure (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, metacarpals, phalanges) but are adapted for different purposes.

Homologous Organs — Same Structure, Different Function

Analogous Organs

Analogous organs have different structural origins but perform the same function. They indicate convergent evolution — different organisms independently evolving similar features due to similar environmental pressures.

Example: Wings of a bird (modified forelimb with bones and feathers) and wings of an insect (membranous extension of exoskeleton). Both are used for flying but have completely different structures and origins.

Other examples: Eyes of an octopus and a human; fins of a fish and flippers of a dolphin.

Analogous Organs — Different Structure, Same Function

Fossils as Evidence for Evolution

Fossils are the preserved remains or impressions of organisms that lived in the past. They provide direct evidence of organisms that once existed and show how life has changed over time.

Fossils help us by:

  • Showing organisms that no longer exist (extinct species).
  • Revealing transitional forms — organisms with features of two different groups (e.g., Archaeopteryx had features of both reptiles and birds).
  • Establishing a timeline of evolution by dating the rock layers (strata) in which fossils are found — deeper layers contain older fossils.

Human Evolution

Modern humans (Homo sapiens) evolved in Africa and have migrated across the globe. Key points from the NCERT text:

  • Humans and other primates (apes, monkeys) share a common ancestor — we did not evolve from modern apes.
  • Tools, fossils, and DNA evidence trace the journey of human migration from Africa to the rest of the world.
  • All races of humans are a single species — there is no biological basis for "superiority" of any race.
  • The evolutionary lineage: early primates → hominids → Homo habilis → Homo erectus → Homo sapiens.

Simplified Human Evolution Timeline

Fun Fact: Archaeopteryx is one of the most famous transitional fossils. It had feathers and a wishbone like birds, but also had teeth, a bony tail, and claws on its wings like reptiles!

Key Takeaways

  • Heredity is the transmission of traits from parents to offspring through genes located on chromosomes.
  • Variation among individuals is the raw material for evolution. It arises from sexual reproduction, mutations, and environmental factors.
  • Mendel's Laws: Law of Dominance, Law of Segregation, and Law of Independent Assortment explain how traits are inherited. Monohybrid cross gives a 3:1 ratio; dihybrid cross gives 9:3:3:1.
  • Sex in humans is determined by the father's sperm: X sperm gives a girl (XX), Y sperm gives a boy (XY).
  • Evolution occurs through accumulation of variations over generations, driven by natural selection, genetic drift, and geographical isolation.
  • Acquired traits (from environment) are NOT inherited. Only inherited traits (gene-based) pass to offspring.
  • Homologous organs (same structure, different function) indicate common ancestry. Analogous organs (different structure, same function) indicate convergent evolution.
  • Fossils provide direct evidence of evolution, showing extinct and transitional organisms.
  • All humans belong to a single species, Homo sapiens, originating from Africa.
