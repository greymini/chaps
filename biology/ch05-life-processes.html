Chapter Overview

Understanding the essential functions that keep living organisms alive and functioning.

What Are Life Processes?

Life processes are the basic functions performed by living organisms to maintain their life. Even when organisms appear to be resting, countless molecular processes keep them alive. These maintenance functions include nutrition, respiration, transportation, and excretion.

The energy needed for life processes comes from the food organisms consume. All living organisms — from single-celled amoeba to complex human beings — perform these processes, though the mechanisms may differ in complexity.

  2. Describe the human digestive system, including the role of each organ and enzyme.

  5. Describe excretion in humans (nephron, dialysis) and plants (waste storage and removal).

Topic 1: Nutrition

How living organisms obtain and utilise food for energy, growth, and repair.

Autotrophic Nutrition

Organisms that synthesise their own food from simple inorganic substances (CO₂ and H₂O) using energy from sunlight are called autotrophs. Green plants are the most common example.

Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll.

6CO₂ + 6H₂O ⟶ C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ In the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll

Role of Chloroplasts

Chloroplasts are organelles in plant cells that contain the pigment chlorophyll. Chlorophyll absorbs sunlight (mainly red and blue wavelengths, reflecting green) and uses this light energy to drive the photosynthesis reaction. The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes and the light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle) occur in the stroma.

Leaf Adaptations for Photosynthesis

  • Broad, flat shape — provides large surface area for absorbing sunlight.
  • Thin structure — allows CO₂ to diffuse easily to all cells.
  • Stomata — tiny pores (mainly on the lower surface) for gas exchange. CO₂ enters and O₂ exits through stomata.
  • Guard cells — sausage-shaped cells flanking each stoma that control opening and closing. When guard cells swell with water, the stoma opens; when they lose water, it closes.
  • Extensive vascular tissue — veins (xylem and phloem) transport water to the leaf and carry away manufactured food.

Heterotrophic Nutrition

Organisms that cannot synthesise their own food and depend on other organisms are called heterotrophs. There are three main types:

1. Holozoic Nutrition

The organism ingests whole food, then digests, absorbs, and assimilates it. The undigested residue is egested.

  • Examples: Humans, dogs, Amoeba (engulfs food with pseudopodia), Paramecium (uses cilia to sweep food into oral groove).
  • Steps: Ingestion → Digestion → Absorption → Assimilation → Egestion

2. Saprophytic Nutrition

The organism feeds on dead and decaying organic matter by secreting digestive enzymes onto the food externally, then absorbing the dissolved nutrients.

  • Examples: Bread mould (Rhizopus), mushrooms, yeast, many bacteria.
  • Importance: Saprophytes are essential decomposers that recycle nutrients back into the ecosystem.

3. Parasitic Nutrition

The organism (parasite) lives on or inside another organism (host) and derives its nutrition from the host, often causing harm.

  • Examples: Cuscuta (dodder plant — wraps around a host plant and absorbs nutrients via haustoria), tapeworm (lives in human intestine), Plasmodium (malaria parasite).

Fun Fact

A single large tree can release about 120 kg of oxygen per year — enough for two people to breathe for an entire year! The Amazon Rainforest alone produces around 20% of the world's oxygen, earning it the nickname "the lungs of the Earth."

Topic 2: Human Digestive System

A step-by-step journey of food through the human alimentary canal.

The Digestive Journey

1. Mouth (Buccal Cavity)

Digestion begins here. Teeth physically break down food (mastication), and the tongue helps mix it with saliva. Salivary amylase (ptyalin) begins the chemical digestion of starch into maltose. The food is shaped into a bolus for easy swallowing.

2. Oesophagus (Food Pipe)

The bolus travels from the mouth to the stomach through the oesophagus via peristalsis — rhythmic wave-like contractions of the muscular walls. No chemical digestion occurs here. The epiglottis prevents food from entering the windpipe.

3. Stomach

The stomach is a J-shaped muscular organ. Its gastric glands secrete:

  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl) — creates an acidic medium (pH ~2), kills bacteria in food, and activates pepsinogen into pepsin.
  • Pepsin — a protease enzyme that breaks down proteins into smaller peptides.
  • Mucus — protects the stomach lining from the corrosive HCl.

Food becomes a semi-liquid acidic mixture called chyme.

4. Small Intestine

The longest part (~6 metres), divided into duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. Most digestion and absorption happens here:

  • Bile (from liver, stored in gall bladder) — emulsifies fats (breaks large fat globules into tiny droplets), making it easier for lipase to act. Bile is not an enzyme.
  • Pancreatic juice (from pancreas) contains:
    • Trypsin — digests proteins into peptides
    • Pancreatic amylase — digests starch into maltose
    • Lipase — digests fats into fatty acids and glycerol
  • Intestinal juice (succus entericus) completes the digestion of all nutrients.

The inner wall of the small intestine has millions of tiny, finger-like projections called villi, which greatly increase the surface area for absorption. Each villus has a network of blood capillaries and a lymph vessel (lacteal) for absorbing nutrients.

5. Large Intestine

The undigested food enters the large intestine where most of the water is absorbed. The remaining material becomes semi-solid faeces, which is stored in the rectum and eliminated through the anus (egestion).

Topic 3: Respiration

The process of breaking down food to release energy for all cellular activities.

Aerobic Respiration

Aerobic respiration occurs in the mitochondria of cells in the presence of oxygen. Glucose is completely oxidised to produce carbon dioxide, water, and a large amount of energy (38 ATP molecules per glucose molecule).

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (ATP) Occurs in mitochondria, in the presence of oxygen

Aerobic respiration is the primary mode of energy production in most organisms, including humans, animals, and plants. It occurs in three stages: glycolysis (in cytoplasm), Krebs cycle (in mitochondrial matrix), and the electron transport chain (on inner mitochondrial membrane).

Anaerobic Respiration

Anaerobic respiration occurs in the absence of oxygen. The glucose molecule is not completely broken down, so less energy is released.

In Yeast (Fermentation)

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + Energy Ethanol (alcohol) + Carbon dioxide — used in baking and brewing

This process is called fermentation. It is used commercially in:

  • Baking — CO₂ produced makes dough rise.
  • Brewing — ethanol produced is the alcohol in wine and beer.

In Human Muscle Cells

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₃H₆O₃ + Energy Lactic acid — causes muscle cramps during vigorous exercise

During intense exercise, when oxygen supply to muscles is insufficient, cells temporarily switch to anaerobic respiration. The accumulation of lactic acid causes muscle fatigue and cramps. This is why you feel soreness after heavy exercise.

Human Respiratory System

The human respiratory system is responsible for taking in oxygen and expelling carbon dioxide:

  1. Nostrils — air enters and is filtered by nasal hair, warmed, and moistened.
  2. Pharynx and Larynx — air passes through the throat to the voice box.
  3. Trachea (windpipe) — lined with C-shaped cartilage rings to prevent collapse; cilia and mucus trap dust particles.
  4. Bronchi — trachea divides into two bronchi, one for each lung.
  5. Bronchioles — bronchi further divide into finer branches within the lungs.
  6. Alveoli — tiny balloon-like structures at the end of bronchioles (about 300 million in each lung). They have:
    • Extremely thin walls (one cell thick) for easy gas exchange.
    • Rich blood supply for efficient O₂/CO₂ exchange.
    • Large total surface area (~70 m²).

Breathing mechanism: The diaphragm and intercostal muscles contract (inhalation) and relax (exhalation) to change the volume of the thoracic cavity, creating pressure differences that move air in and out of the lungs.

Topic 4: Transportation

How nutrients, gases, and waste products are moved within the bodies of humans and plants.

Transportation in Humans

Blood Components

  • Plasma — the liquid portion (~55% of blood volume); transports dissolved nutrients, hormones, CO₂, and waste products. It is pale yellow in colour.
  • Red Blood Cells (RBCs / Erythrocytes) — contain haemoglobin, a red iron-containing pigment that binds with oxygen to form oxyhaemoglobin. RBCs are biconcave disc-shaped and lack a nucleus in mammals.
  • White Blood Cells (WBCs / Leukocytes) — part of the immune system; they fight infections and foreign invaders. Types include neutrophils, lymphocytes, and monocytes.
  • Platelets (Thrombocytes) — cell fragments that help in blood clotting (coagulation) at wound sites, preventing excessive blood loss.

The Heart

The human heart is a muscular organ with 4 chambers:

  • Right atrium — receives deoxygenated blood from the body via the vena cava.
  • Right ventricle — pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery.
  • Left atrium — receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via pulmonary veins.
  • Left ventricle — pumps oxygenated blood to the whole body via the aorta. It has the thickest walls because it needs to generate the highest pressure.

Valves (tricuspid, bicuspid/mitral, and semilunar) ensure blood flows in one direction only, preventing backflow.

Double Circulation

Blood passes through the heart twice in one complete cycle:

  1. Pulmonary circulation: Right ventricle → Lungs (oxygenation) → Left atrium
  2. Systemic circulation: Left ventricle → Body organs (delivers O₂) → Right atrium

Double circulation ensures that oxygenated and deoxygenated blood do not mix, which is essential for the high energy demands of warm-blooded (homeothermic) animals.

Blood Vessels

  • Arteries — carry blood away from the heart; thick, elastic walls; blood flows under high pressure; no valves (except semilunar).
  • Veins — carry blood towards the heart; thinner walls; blood flows under low pressure; contain valves to prevent backflow.
  • Capillaries — extremely thin-walled (one cell thick); allow exchange of substances between blood and tissues.

Lymph (Tissue Fluid)

Some plasma, proteins, and white blood cells escape from capillaries into intercellular spaces, forming lymph. The lymphatic system drains this fluid back into the blood. Lymph also carries digested fats from the intestine and contains lymphocytes that help fight infections.

Transportation in Plants

Xylem — Water and Mineral Transport

Xylem tissue transports water and dissolved minerals upward from roots to leaves. This is a unidirectional process driven by:

  • Root pressure — osmotic pressure in root cells pushes water upward.
  • Transpiration pull — the main driving force. As water evaporates from leaf stomata (transpiration), it creates a suction force that pulls water upward through the xylem vessels like a continuous column. This is explained by the cohesion-tension theory.

Xylem vessels are made of dead cells with lignified (hardened) walls, forming a continuous hollow tube from roots to leaves.

Phloem — Food Transport (Translocation)

Phloem tissue transports prepared food (sucrose and amino acids) from leaves (source) to all other parts of the plant (sink). This is called translocation and can be bidirectional.

  • Phloem is made of living cells: sieve tubes and companion cells.
  • Translocation requires energy (ATP) to load sucrose into the sieve tubes, creating a high osmotic pressure that drives the flow of sap.

Fun Fact

If you laid all the blood vessels in the human body end to end, they would stretch approximately 100,000 kilometres — that is enough to circle the Earth about 2.5 times! Your heart beats around 100,000 times every day, pumping about 7,500 litres of blood.

Topic 5: Excretion

The removal of metabolic waste products from the body to maintain homeostasis.

Excretion in Humans

The human excretory system consists of a pair of kidneys, two ureters, a urinary bladder, and a urethra.

Kidneys

The kidneys are bean-shaped organs located on either side of the spine, just below the rib cage. Each kidney contains about one million nephrons — the functional units that filter blood and produce urine.

Nephron Structure and Function

Each nephron has two main parts: the Bowman's capsule (containing the glomerulus) and a long renal tubule.

  1. Filtration (at the Glomerulus): Blood enters the kidney through the renal artery, which branches into tiny capillaries forming a bundle called the glomerulus inside the Bowman's capsule. High blood pressure forces water, salts, glucose, amino acids, and urea out of the blood into the Bowman's capsule. This filtrate is called glomerular filtrate. Blood cells and large proteins remain in the blood.
  2. Reabsorption (in the Tubule): As the filtrate passes through the proximal tubule, loop of Henle, and distal tubule, useful substances like glucose, amino acids, water, and ions are selectively reabsorbed back into the surrounding blood capillaries. About 99% of the filtrate is reabsorbed.
  3. Secretion: Some substances like excess H⁺ ions, K⁺ ions, and certain drugs are actively secreted from the blood into the tubular fluid.

The remaining fluid (urine) contains water, urea, uric acid, and excess salts. It flows from the collecting ducts into the ureter and is stored in the urinary bladder before being expelled through the urethra.

Dialysis

When kidneys fail (renal failure), a machine called a dialyser (artificial kidney) is used to filter the patient's blood. This process is called haemodialysis. Blood is drawn from an artery, passed through a dialysis membrane immersed in a dialysing fluid (which has the same concentration of salts and glucose as normal blood but no urea), and the cleaned blood is returned to a vein. Dialysis must be done regularly (typically 3 times a week) until a kidney transplant is possible.

Excretion in Plants

Plants do not have a specialised excretory system like animals, but they have several strategies to deal with waste products:

  • Oxygen (produced during photosynthesis) is released through stomata. This is a waste product of photosynthesis but is used in respiration.
  • Carbon dioxide (produced during respiration) is released through stomata or reused in photosynthesis.
  • Excess water is lost through transpiration via stomata and through guttation (water droplets at leaf margins).
  • Some waste products are stored in cellular vacuoles, in ageing leaves, or in the bark of trees. These are removed when leaves fall off (leaf abscission) or bark peels away.
  • Some plants store wastes as resins, gums, and latex in old xylem.
  • Plants also excrete some waste products into the soil through their roots.

Summary & Key Takeaways

Key Equations at a Glance

Photosynthesis: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O ⟶ C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ Sunlight + Chlorophyll
Aerobic Respiration: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (ATP)
Anaerobic (Yeast): C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + Energy
Anaerobic (Muscles): C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₃H₆O₃ (Lactic Acid) + Energy

Nutrition

  • Autotrophs make their own food (photosynthesis).
  • Heterotrophs depend on others: holozoic, saprophytic, parasitic.
  • Stomata and guard cells control gas exchange in leaves.

Digestion

  • Alimentary canal: mouth to anus with specialised enzymes at each stage.
  • Salivary amylase, pepsin, bile, trypsin, lipase work on different nutrients.
  • Villi in the small intestine maximise absorption surface area.

Respiration

  • Aerobic: complete oxidation, 38 ATP, in mitochondria.
  • Anaerobic: incomplete breakdown, less energy, no O₂ needed.
  • Alveoli in the lungs provide large surface area for gas exchange.

Transportation

  • Blood: plasma, RBCs, WBCs, platelets — each has specific roles.
  • Heart: 4 chambers, double circulation prevents mixing.
  • Xylem (water up, transpiration pull) and phloem (food, bidirectional).

Excretion

  • Kidneys filter blood; nephrons perform filtration, reabsorption, secretion.
  • Dialysis substitutes kidney function in renal failure.
  • Plants store waste in leaves/bark and remove during leaf fall.

Key Differences

  • Arteries (from heart, thick) vs. Veins (to heart, valves).
  • Xylem (dead cells, unidirectional) vs. Phloem (living, bidirectional).
  • Aerobic (O₂, more ATP) vs. Anaerobic (no O₂, less ATP).

Key Terms

Photosynthesis — food synthesis by plants using sunlight
Chlorophyll — green pigment that absorbs light energy
Stomata — tiny pores on leaf surface for gas exchange
Peristalsis — wave-like muscle contractions in the alimentary canal
Villi — finger-like projections increasing absorption area
Alveoli — air sacs in lungs for gas exchange
Haemoglobin — oxygen-carrying pigment in RBCs
Double circulation — blood passes through heart twice per cycle
Transpiration — water loss from leaves creating pulling force
Translocation — transport of food by phloem
Nephron — functional unit of the kidney

Dialysis — artificial blood filtering for kidney failure
Fermentation — anaerobic respiration in yeast
Osmoregulation — regulation of water and ion balance
