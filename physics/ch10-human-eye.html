Chapter Overview

How we see, why the sky is blue, and the fascinating science of light and the human eye.

How Do We See?

We see objects when light reflected from them enters our eyes. The light passes through the cornea, pupil, and crystalline lens, which together refract (bend) it to form a sharp, inverted, real image on the retina. The retina contains light-sensitive cells (rods and cones) that convert the image into electrical signals. These signals travel via the optic nerve to the brain, which interprets the image as upright.

This chapter covers the structure and working of the human eye, common vision defects and their correction, the behaviour of light through prisms, atmospheric phenomena, and the scattering of light.

The Human Eye — Structure & Working

A remarkable optical instrument that uses a convex lens system to focus light on the retina.

Parts of the Human Eye

  • Cornea — Transparent, curved front surface. Performs most of the refraction of incoming light.
  • Aqueous Humour — Clear fluid between the cornea and the lens. Nourishes the eye and maintains pressure.
  • Iris — Coloured, muscular diaphragm that controls the size of the pupil. Gives the eye its colour.
  • Pupil — The aperture (opening) in the iris. Adjusts size to regulate the amount of light entering: dilates in dim light, constricts in bright light.
  • Crystalline Lens — A transparent, biconvex lens that provides fine focusing. It can change shape (become thicker or thinner) to focus on objects at different distances.
  • Ciliary Muscles — Ring of muscles attached to the lens. They contract or relax to change the shape (and hence focal length) of the lens.
  • Vitreous Humour — Transparent, jelly-like substance filling the large chamber behind the lens. Maintains the shape of the eyeball.
  • Retina — The screen at the back of the eye. Contains light-sensitive cells: rods (dim light, black-and-white vision) and cones (bright light, colour vision).
  • Optic Nerve — Carries electrical signals from the retina to the brain for interpretation.
  • Blind Spot — The point where the optic nerve exits the retina. No photoreceptors here, so no image is formed at this spot.

How the Eye Works

Light from an object enters the eye in this sequence:

Light → Cornea (major refraction) → Aqueous Humour → Pupil → Crystalline Lens (fine focus) → Vitreous Humour → Retina An inverted, real, diminished image forms on the retina

The retina converts the light image into electrical nerve impulses. The optic nerve transmits these signals to the brain, which processes and interprets the image as erect (upright). The impression of an image on the retina lasts for about 1/16th of a second — this is called persistence of vision, which is why we perceive movies as continuous motion.

Power of Accommodation

The ability of the eye to change the focal length of its lens to focus on objects at varying distances is called the power of accommodation.

  • Viewing distant objects (∞): Ciliary muscles relax → lens becomes thin → focal length increases → parallel rays from distant objects focus exactly on the retina.
  • Viewing near objects: Ciliary muscles contract → lens becomes thick → focal length decreases → diverging rays from near objects are brought to focus on the retina.
Key Definitions

Near Point (D): The closest distance at which the eye can see an object clearly = 25 cm for a normal eye. Also called the least distance of distinct vision.

Far Point: The farthest point up to which the eye can see clearly = infinity (∞) for a normal eye.

Limits of Accommodation

The accommodation ability of the eye has limits. The crystalline lens cannot become infinitely thick or thin. When the eye is focused on the near point, the ciliary muscles are maximally contracted and the lens is at its thickest — the eye is most strained. When focused at the far point (infinity), the muscles are fully relaxed and the lens is at its thinnest — the eye is most relaxed. This is why we find it comfortable to look at distant objects for long periods but reading up close for a long time causes eye strain.

Range of accommodation = Far point − Near point = ∞ − 25 cm For a normal eye, this range spans from 25 cm to infinity

Rods and Cones — The Photoreceptors

The retina contains two types of light-sensitive cells that act as photoreceptors:

  • Rods (~120 million per eye): Extremely sensitive to light. Responsible for dim-light (scotopic) vision and peripheral vision. They detect brightness but cannot distinguish colours, so we see in black-and-white in very dim light.
  • Cones (~6 million per eye): Responsible for bright-light (photopic) vision and colour vision. Three types of cones detect red, green, and blue light respectively. Concentrated at the fovea (yellow spot), which is the point of sharpest vision.

When light strikes these cells, they generate electrical signals proportional to the intensity and wavelength of the light. These signals are carried by the optic nerve to the visual cortex of the brain, where the image is interpreted.

Defects of Vision & Correction

Understanding myopia, hypermetropia, and presbyopia — and how corrective lenses restore clear vision.

Myopia (Near-sightedness)

A person with myopia can see nearby objects clearly but cannot see distant objects sharply. The far point of the myopic eye is closer than infinity.

Causes

  • Elongation of the eyeball (eyeball becomes too long)
  • Excessive curvature of the cornea or lens (converging power too high)

What Happens?

The image of a distant object is formed in front of the retina, not on it.

Correction

A concave (diverging) lens of suitable focal length is used. It diverges the incoming rays slightly before they enter the eye, so the image shifts back onto the retina.

f = −d (far point of the defective eye) where d is the distance of the far point from the eye, and f is negative (concave lens)

Hypermetropia (Far-sightedness)

A person with hypermetropia can see distant objects clearly but cannot see nearby objects distinctly. The near point of the hypermetropic eye is farther than 25 cm.

Causes

  • Shortening of the eyeball (eyeball too short)
  • Focal length of the lens too long (converging power too low)

What Happens?

The image of a nearby object is formed behind the retina.

Correction

A convex (converging) lens of appropriate focal length is used. It converges the incoming rays slightly before they enter the eye, so the image shifts forward onto the retina.

Using lens formula: 1/f = 1/v − 1/u where v = −25 cm (image at near point of normal eye), u = −N (near point of defective eye)

Presbyopia

An age-related condition where the eye gradually loses its power of accommodation. The ciliary muscles weaken and the crystalline lens becomes increasingly rigid with age.

Symptoms

Difficulty in seeing nearby objects clearly (reading, sewing). The near point recedes farther and farther from the eye. Sometimes a person may have both myopia and hypermetropia simultaneously.

Correction

A bifocal lens is prescribed. The upper portion is a concave lens for distance vision, and the lower portion is a convex lens for reading. If only near vision is affected, simple convex reading glasses suffice.

Progression with Age

The near point of the eye changes with age:

  • Age 10: Near point ≈ 7 cm (very flexible lens, strong accommodation)
  • Age 25: Near point ≈ 25 cm (normal, standard near point)
  • Age 40: Near point ≈ 40 cm (presbyopia begins)
  • Age 60: Near point ≈ 200 cm (severe presbyopia, reading glasses essential)

Power of the Corrective Lens

The power of a lens is defined as the reciprocal of its focal length (in metres). It is measured in dioptres (D).

P = 1/f (where f is in metres) Concave lens: f is negative, so P is negative. Convex lens: f is positive, so P is positive.

Worked Example 1: Myopia

A myopic person has a far point of 200 cm. Find the power of the corrective lens.

f = −200 cm = −2.0 m
P = 1/f = 1/(−2.0) = −0.5 D
A concave lens of power −0.5 D is needed.

Worked Example 2: Hypermetropia

A hypermetropic person has a near point of 100 cm. Find the power of the corrective lens.

v = −100 cm, u = −25 cm
1/f = 1/v − 1/u = 1/(−100) − 1/(−25) = −0.01 + 0.04 = 0.03
f = 1/0.03 = 33.33 cm = 0.333 m
P = 1/0.333 = +3.0 D
A convex lens of power +3.0 D is needed.

Refraction Through a Prism

How a prism bends light and reveals the hidden colours within white light.

The Prism

A prism is a transparent optical medium bounded by two plane surfaces (called refracting surfaces) inclined at an angle called the angle of the prism (A). It has a triangular cross-section.

When a ray of white light enters a prism:

  1. It bends toward the base at the first surface (entering denser medium).
  2. It bends toward the base again at the second surface (exiting to rarer medium).
  3. The angle of deviation (δ) is the angle between the original incident ray and the final emergent ray.

Dispersion of White Light

The splitting of white light into its component colours is called dispersion. This happens because different colours (wavelengths) of light have different refractive indices in the glass. Violet light bends the most (highest refractive index) and red light bends the least (lowest refractive index).

Remember

VIBGYOR: Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red — from most deviated (violet) to least deviated (red). Isaac Newton was the first to demonstrate that white light is made up of seven colours using a glass prism.

Recombination of White Light

Newton showed that if the dispersed spectrum from the first prism is passed through a second identical prism placed in an inverted position, the colours recombine to form white light. This proves that white light consists of seven colours, and the prism does not add any colour — it merely separates what already exists.

Newton's Experiment

Newton used a narrow slit to isolate a single colour from the dispersed spectrum and passed it through a second prism. The colour did not split further — proving that individual colours of the spectrum are pure and cannot be further decomposed. He called these the “spectrum” of white light.

Wavelengths of Visible Light

The visible spectrum spans wavelengths from approximately 380 nm (violet) to 750 nm (red). Each colour corresponds to a specific range of wavelengths:

ColourWavelength Range (nm)Refractive Index (glass)Deviation
Violet380 – 450HighestMaximum
Indigo450 – 475↓↓

The speed of light in a medium depends on its wavelength. Since violet light has the shortest wavelength, it slows down the most in glass and bends the most. Red light, with the longest wavelength, bends the least.

Atmospheric Refraction

How the Earth's atmosphere bends light and creates fascinating optical phenomena.

Twinkling of Stars

Stars appear to twinkle because of atmospheric refraction. Starlight passes through the Earth's atmosphere, which has layers of air with varying densities and temperatures. As these layers shift continuously, the refractive index changes, causing the apparent position of the star to fluctuate slightly. This makes the star appear to twinkle — its brightness and position seem to change rapidly.

Why don't planets twinkle?

Planets are much closer to Earth than stars, so they have a larger angular size (they appear as tiny discs, not points). The light from different edges of a planet averages out the fluctuations, so the twinkling effect is negligible.

Advance Sunrise and Delayed Sunset

The Sun is visible to us about 2 minutes before the actual sunrise and about 2 minutes after the actual sunset due to atmospheric refraction. The atmosphere bends the sunlight toward the Earth's surface as it passes through layers of increasing density.

As a result, the apparent position of the Sun is slightly higher than its actual position when it is near the horizon. This makes the day approximately 4 minutes longer than it would be without atmospheric refraction.

Extra daylight ≈ 2 min (early sunrise) + 2 min (delayed sunset) = ~4 minutes

Also, the Sun appears flattened (oval) at sunrise and sunset because refraction is stronger near the horizon — the lower edge of the Sun is raised more than the upper edge.

Think About It

The extra 4 minutes of daylight mean that on the equinox (when day and night should theoretically be exactly 12 hours each), the day is actually slightly longer than 12 hours and the night slightly shorter. The Sun's disc itself takes about 2 minutes to cross the horizon, so the combined effect of disc size and refraction makes equinox days noticeably longer than the 12-hour ideal.

Mirage

A mirage is an optical illusion that occurs in hot regions (like deserts or on a hot road). It makes distant objects appear to have a shimmering reflection below them, as if there were a pool of water.

How it forms:

  1. On a hot day, the air near the ground is much hotter and less dense than the air above.
  2. This creates a gradient of decreasing refractive index from top to bottom.
  3. Light from a distant object (like a tree or the sky) bends progressively as it passes through these layers.
  4. At a certain angle, total internal reflection occurs, and the light curves upward toward the observer.
  5. The observer sees an inverted image below the object, which appears like a reflection in water.

Superior Mirage (Looming)

In cold regions (like over ice or cold water), the reverse happens: air near the surface is colder and denser, while the air above is warmer. Light from distant objects bends downward, and the observer sees objects that are actually below the horizon appearing above their actual position (or floating in the air). This is called a superior mirage or looming, and it explains some historical reports of “floating ships” at sea.

How Atmospheric Refraction Works

The Earth's atmosphere is made up of layers of air with varying optical densities. The air near the surface is denser and warmer; higher layers are cooler and less dense. When light from a celestial object (star, Sun, Moon) enters the atmosphere, it passes through these layers and gets refracted continuously, bending toward the normal at each layer as it enters denser air.

This continuous refraction means the apparent position of the object is slightly higher than its actual position. The effect is most pronounced when the object is near the horizon, as the light has to pass through more layers.

Apparent position of a star or the Sun is always higher than its actual position due to atmospheric refraction. The bending is toward the normal (toward denser layers) at each stage.

Why the Sun appears flattened at the horizon

The refraction of light from the bottom edge of the Sun is slightly more than from the top edge (as the bottom edge passes through denser atmosphere). This lifts the bottom edge more than the top, compressing the Sun's disc vertically and making it appear oval or flattened near the horizon.

Scattering of Light

Why the sky is blue, sunsets are red, and why danger signals use red colour.

Tyndall Effect

The scattering of light by colloidal particles (such as dust, smoke, tiny water droplets) is called the Tyndall effect. It makes the path of a light beam visible. The phenomenon is named after the 19th-century physicist John Tyndall.

Everyday Examples:

  • A beam of sunlight entering a dusty room through a window — the path becomes visible due to scattering by dust particles.
  • Headlight beams visible in fog or mist.
  • The blue colour of smoke from a burning incense stick (fine particles scatter shorter wavelengths).
  • The bluish tinge of water seen in a clear glass when a few drops of milk are added (milk particles scatter blue light).
  • Searchlight beams visible in the night sky due to dust and moisture particles.

Conditions for Tyndall Effect

The Tyndall effect is observed when:

  • The size of the scattering particles is comparable to or slightly larger than the wavelength of light (in the colloidal range: 1 nm to 1000 nm).
  • The particles are suspended in a transparent medium (air, water, glass).

The colour of the scattered light depends on the size of the particles. Very fine particles scatter shorter wavelengths (blue) more, while larger particles scatter all wavelengths roughly equally (appearing white, as in fog).

Nature's Tyndall Effect

The beautiful blue colour seen in the eyes of some people (especially babies) is partly due to the Tyndall effect — fine colloidal particles in the iris scatter blue light preferentially. Similarly, the blue colour of some bird feathers and butterfly wings is structural (scattering-based) rather than pigment-based.

Why is the Sky Blue?

The molecules of the atmosphere (N₂, O₂) are much smaller than the wavelength of visible light. They scatter shorter wavelengths much more effectively than longer wavelengths. This is called Rayleigh scattering.

Intensity of scattering ∝ 1 / λ⁴ Shorter wavelength = scattered much more

Blue light (λ ≈ 470 nm) is scattered about 5.5 times more than red light (λ ≈ 620 nm). Violet is scattered even more, but our eyes are more sensitive to blue, and some violet is absorbed by the upper atmosphere. So the sky appears blue.

Why is the Sun Red at Sunrise and Sunset?

At sunrise and sunset, the sunlight must travel through the maximum thickness of the atmosphere to reach our eyes. During this long journey, most of the shorter wavelengths (blue, violet) get scattered away in all directions. Only the longer wavelengths (red, orange) remain and reach our eyes, making the Sun and the sky near the horizon appear reddish-orange.

Why Does the Sun Appear White at Noon?

At noon, the Sun is overhead and light travels the shortest path through the atmosphere. Very little scattering occurs, so all colours reach our eyes almost equally, and the Sun appears white (or yellowish-white).

Why Are Danger Signals Red?

Red colour is used for danger signals (traffic lights, brake lights, fire engines) because red light is scattered the least by fog, smoke, and atmospheric particles (since it has the longest wavelength among visible colours). This means red light can travel the farthest distance without much loss of intensity, making it visible from far away even in poor conditions.

Interesting Connection

If the Earth had no atmosphere at all, the sky would appear black (as seen by astronauts from space or on the Moon), and we would see stars even during the day. It is the atmosphere's scattering that creates the beautiful blue dome we call the sky. Similarly, if atmospheric particles were much larger (comparable to the wavelength of light), all colours would scatter equally, and the sky would appear white — this is exactly what happens on an overcast or foggy day.

Colour of the Sun at Different Altitudes

The apparent colour of the Sun changes throughout the day based on how much atmosphere the light must traverse:

  • Sunrise / Sunset (near horizon): Maximum atmospheric path → blue and violet completely scattered away → Sun appears reddish-orange.
  • Mid-morning / Late afternoon: Moderate atmospheric path → some blue scattered → Sun appears yellowish.
  • Noon (directly overhead): Minimum atmospheric path → negligible scattering → Sun appears white.
  • From space (no atmosphere): No scattering at all → Sun appears white against a black sky.

Common Misconceptions

  • Misconception: The crystalline lens does all the refraction.
    Fact: The cornea performs most (~2/3) of the refraction. The lens provides fine adjustments for focusing.
  • Misconception: The sky is blue because it reflects the ocean.
    Fact: The blue colour is due to Rayleigh scattering of sunlight by atmospheric molecules. Even deserts have blue skies.
  • Misconception: Violet should be the dominant sky colour since it scatters most.
    Fact: Human eyes are more sensitive to blue, and some violet is absorbed by the upper atmosphere. So the sky appears blue, not violet.
  • Misconception: Stars twinkle because they emit light in pulses.
    Fact: Stars emit light continuously. The twinkling is caused by atmospheric refraction changing the apparent position and brightness.
  • Misconception: Presbyopia is the same as hypermetropia.
    Fact: Presbyopia is age-related loss of accommodation (weakening ciliary muscles), while hypermetropia is a structural defect (eyeball too short). A person can have both simultaneously.
