Chapter Overview

Understanding the fundamental concepts of electric charge, current, circuits, and their practical applications.

What is Electricity?

Electricity is a form of energy resulting from the flow of electric charge. It powers nearly everything in our daily lives — from light bulbs and fans to computers and factories. This chapter covers electric current, potential difference, Ohm's law, resistance, circuit combinations, heating effects, and electric power.

Understanding electricity begins with understanding electric charge. All matter is made of atoms, which contain positively charged protons and negatively charged electrons. When electrons flow through a conductor, we get electric current.

Electric Current & Circuit

Understanding the flow of charges and the path they travel.

Electric Charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. It comes in two types: positive (protons) and negative (electrons). The SI unit of charge is the Coulomb (C).

Charge of one electron = 1.6 × 10¹&sup9; C 1 Coulomb = charge of 6.25 × 10¹&sup8; electrons

Like charges repel each other, while unlike charges attract. In a conductor, the free electrons are the charge carriers that constitute electric current.

Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor. If a charge Q flows through a conductor in time t, then:

I = Q / t Where I = current (Ampere), Q = charge (Coulomb), t = time (second)

The SI unit of current is the Ampere (A). One ampere means one coulomb of charge flowing per second: 1 A = 1 C / 1 s.

Smaller units: milliampere (mA) = 10⁻³ A, microampere (μA) = 10⁻&sup6; A.

Direction of Current

  • Conventional current — flows from positive (+) terminal to negative (−) terminal of the battery (outside the cell). This was historically defined before the discovery of electrons.
  • Electron flow — actual flow of electrons is from negative (−) to positive (+) terminal, opposite to conventional current.

Electric Circuit

An electric circuit is a closed continuous path through which electric current flows. A circuit must have:

  • Source of energy — cell or battery (provides potential difference)
  • Conducting wires — pathway for current
  • Load/device — bulb, resistor, motor, etc.
  • Switch — to open or close the circuit

Fun Fact

When you switch on a light, the electric signal (electromagnetic wave) travels at nearly the speed of light (~3 × 10⁸ m/s), even though the actual electrons drift very slowly at about 0.1 mm/s! It is the electric field that propagates almost instantly.

Electric Potential & Potential Difference

The driving force that pushes charges through a circuit.

Electric Potential

Electric potential at a point is the work done per unit positive charge in bringing a small test charge from infinity to that point.

The potential difference (V) between two points is the work done (W) to move a unit charge (Q) from one point to another:

V = W / Q Where V = potential difference (Volt), W = work done (Joule), Q = charge (Coulomb)

The SI unit is the Volt (V). One volt means one joule of work done per coulomb of charge: 1 V = 1 J / 1 C.

Measuring Instruments

Voltmeter

  • Measures potential difference between two points
  • Connected in parallel across the component
  • Has very high resistance (so it draws negligible current)

Ammeter

  • Measures electric current flowing through a component
  • Connected in series with the component
  • Has very low resistance (so it does not change the current)
Remember

Ammeter → Along (series). Voltmeter → aVoid (parallel, avoids the main path). An ammeter in parallel can cause a short circuit!

Ohm's Law

The fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.

Statement of Ohm's Law

At constant temperature, the potential difference (V) across the ends of a conductor is directly proportional to the electric current (I) flowing through it.

V = I R Where V = potential difference (V), I = current (A), R = resistance (Ω)

The V-I graph for an ohmic conductor is a straight line passing through the origin. The slope of this line gives the resistance R.

Resistance

Resistance is the opposition to the flow of current in a conductor. It is the ratio of potential difference to current:

R = V / I Unit: Ohm (Ω). 1 Ω = 1 V / 1 A

Factors Affecting Resistance

The resistance of a conductor depends on:

R = ρl / A Where ρ = resistivity (Ω·m), l = length (m), A = cross-sectional area (m²)
  • Length (l) — R is directly proportional to length. Longer wire = more resistance.
  • Cross-sectional area (A) — R is inversely proportional to area. Thicker wire = less resistance.
  • Material (resistivity ρ) — depends on the nature of the material.
  • Temperature — for metals, resistance increases with temperature.

Resistivity (ρ)

Resistivity is a property of the material itself, independent of the dimensions of the conductor. Its SI unit is Ω·m (ohm-metre).

Material TypeExamplesResistivity (Ω·m)
Conductors (metals)Silver, Copper, Aluminium10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁶
AlloysNichrome, Constantan, Manganin10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁵
SemiconductorsSilicon, Germanium10⁻⁵ to 10⁶
InsulatorsRubber, Glass, Ebonite10¹² to 10¹⁷

Alloys like nichrome, constantan, and manganin are used in heating elements because they have high resistivity, do not oxidize easily at high temperatures, and their resistance changes very little with temperature.

Fun Fact

Georg Simon Ohm published his law in 1827 but was initially ridiculed by the scientific community. It took nearly 20 years for his work to be fully accepted. Today, the unit of resistance (Ω) is named in his honour!

Resistance in Series & Parallel

How resistors combine to change the total resistance of a circuit.

Series Combination

When resistors are connected end-to-end, the same current flows through each resistor. The total voltage divides across them.

Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ Same current: I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃  |  Voltage divides: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
  • Total resistance is always greater than the largest individual resistance.
  • If one component fails, the entire circuit breaks (like old Christmas lights).
  • Current is the same everywhere in the circuit.

Parallel Combination

When resistors are connected across the same two points, each resistor gets the full voltage. The total current divides among them.

1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ Same voltage: V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃  |  Current divides: I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃
  • Total resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance.
  • If one component fails, others continue to work (household wiring).
  • Each device gets the full voltage of the supply.

Heating Effect of Electric Current

When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted to heat.

Joule's Law of Heating

When electric current passes through a conductor of resistance R for time t, heat is produced:

H = I²Rt Also: H = VIt = V²t/R (using Ohm's law substitutions)

The heat produced is:

  • Directly proportional to the square of current (I²)
  • Directly proportional to the resistance (R)
  • Directly proportional to the time (t)

Applications of Heating Effect

  • Electric heater, iron, toaster — use a high-resistance coil (usually nichrome) that gets very hot when current flows.
  • Electric bulb — thin tungsten filament (melting point ~3380°C) heated to white-hot, emitting light.
  • Electric fuse — safety device made of alloy of lead and tin (low melting point). Placed in series. If current exceeds the rated value, the fuse wire melts and breaks the circuit.

Fuse ratings: 1A, 2A, 3A, 5A, 10A, 15A. Choose a fuse slightly above the normal operating current of the device.

Electric Power & Energy

Understanding how electricity is measured, consumed, and billed.

Electric Power

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or dissipated in a circuit.

P = V I = I²R = V²/R Unit: Watt (W). 1 W = 1 V × 1 A = 1 J/s

Larger units: kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W. A 100 W bulb consumes energy at 100 joules per second.

Electric Energy & Commercial Unit

Electric energy consumed equals power multiplied by time:

E = P × t = V I t SI unit: Joule (J). Commercial unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh)
1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 10⁶ J 1 kWh = 1 "unit" on your electricity bill

Calculating Electricity Bill

Your monthly electricity bill is calculated as:

Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) × Time (hours)
Cost = Energy (kWh) × Rate per unit (₹/kWh)

Example: A 1000 W heater runs for 2 hours daily for 30 days at ₹5/unit.

Energy = 1 kW × 2 h × 30 = 60 kWh
Cost = 60 × ₹5 = ₹300
