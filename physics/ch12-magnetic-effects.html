Chapter Overview

Understanding the deep connection between electricity and magnetism — two sides of the same fundamental force.

Electricity and Magnetism: A Historic Link

Magnetism has been known since ancient times — the Greeks discovered that a naturally occurring mineral called lodestone (magnetite, Fe₃O₄) could attract iron. For centuries, magnets and electricity were thought to be separate phenomena.

In 1820, Danish physicist Hans Christian Oersted made a groundbreaking discovery: a compass needle placed near a current-carrying wire was deflected. This proved that electric current produces a magnetic field, unifying electricity and magnetism into a single force — electromagnetism.

Magnetic Field and Field Lines

The invisible force field around every magnet, mapped by field lines.

What Is a Magnetic Field?

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet (or current-carrying conductor) where its magnetic influence can be detected. It is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction. The direction of the magnetic field at any point is the direction in which a north pole of a compass needle would point.

The SI unit of magnetic field strength is Tesla (T). The Earth itself has a magnetic field (approximately 25–65 μT), with its magnetic south pole near the geographic north pole.

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines

  1. Direction: Field lines emerge from the North pole and enter the South pole outside the magnet. Inside the magnet, they travel from S to N, forming closed loops.
  2. Never cross: Two field lines never intersect each other. If they did, the compass needle would point in two directions at the same point, which is impossible.
  3. Density = Strength: Closer (denser) field lines indicate a stronger magnetic field. Field lines are closest at the poles where the field is strongest.
  4. Tangent rule: The tangent drawn at any point on a field line gives the direction of the magnetic field at that point.
Fun Fact

The Earth's magnetic poles do not coincide with the geographic poles. The magnetic south pole is near the geographic North Pole. This is why a compass needle (which points to magnetic south of Earth, but labelled "N" on the compass) points approximately toward geographic north!

Magnetic Field Due to Current-Carrying Conductor

Oersted's experiment and the field patterns of wires, loops, and solenoids.

Oersted's Experiment (1820)

Oersted placed a magnetic compass near a straight conductor. When current flowed through the wire, the compass needle was deflected, proving that electric current produces a magnetic field. When the current was reversed, the needle deflected in the opposite direction. When the current was switched off, the needle returned to its original position.

Electric Current → Magnetic Field around the conductor The field exists only as long as current flows

Right-Hand Thumb Rule

For a straight current-carrying conductor, the magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire. The direction is given by the right-hand thumb rule: if you wrap your right hand around the wire with the thumb pointing in the direction of current, your curled fingers show the direction of the magnetic field.

Field of a Circular Loop / Coil

When current flows through a circular loop, the magnetic field lines emerge from one face and enter the other — making it behave like a flat magnet. At the centre of the loop, the field lines are nearly straight and parallel.

  • More turns in the coil = stronger magnetic field at the centre.
  • Greater current = stronger field.
  • Smaller radius = stronger field at the centre.

Solenoid — A Coil of Many Turns

A solenoid is a long coil of insulated wire wound in the form of a cylinder. When current flows through it:

  • The field inside is strong, uniform, and parallel (like a bar magnet).
  • The field outside is identical to a bar magnet — field lines go from the N-pole end to the S-pole end.
  • One end acts as North pole, the other as South pole.
Right-Hand Rule for Solenoid:
Curl the fingers of the right hand in the direction of current → Thumb points toward the North pole

Electromagnet

A solenoid with a soft iron core — a temporary, controllable magnet.

What Is an Electromagnet?

An electromagnet is a solenoid with a soft iron core placed inside it. The soft iron core greatly enhances the magnetic field because iron is easily magnetised. It is a temporary magnet — it loses its magnetism as soon as the current is switched off.

Advantages Over Permanent Magnets

  • Can be switched on/off — by controlling the current.
  • Strength can be varied — by changing the current or the number of turns.
  • Polarity can be reversed — by reversing the direction of current.

Applications

  • Scrap-yard cranes — lift and release heavy iron/steel objects.
  • MRI machines — use powerful electromagnets to create medical images.
  • Electric bells — electromagnet attracts and releases a hammer repeatedly.
  • Loudspeakers — electromagnet vibrates a diaphragm to produce sound.
  • Maglev trains — electromagnets enable frictionless levitation.
Fun Fact

The world's strongest continuous-field electromagnet, at the National High Magnetic Field Laboratory (Florida, USA), can produce a field of 45 Tesla — about a million times stronger than Earth's magnetic field!

Force on Current-Carrying Conductor in Magnetic Field

Fleming's left-hand rule, the force equation, and the electric motor.

The Motor Effect

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a force. This is called the motor effect. The force is:

  • Maximum when the current is perpendicular to the magnetic field.
  • Zero when the current is parallel to the magnetic field.
F = B × I × L F = force (N), B = magnetic field (T), I = current (A), L = length of conductor in field (m)

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule (Motor Rule)

Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of your left hand mutually perpendicular to each other:

Thumb = Force (F) Forefinger = Field (B) Middle = Current (I)
Thumb = Motion Forefinger = Field (B) Middle = Induced I

Electric Motor (DC Motor)

An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy (rotation). It is based on the principle that a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force.

How the DC Motor Works

  1. Current flows through the rectangular coil ABCD placed in a magnetic field (between N and S poles).
  2. Side AB experiences an upward force and side CD experiences a downward force (by Fleming's left-hand rule).
  3. These equal and opposite forces create a torque that rotates the coil.
  4. After half a rotation, the split-ring commutator reverses the current direction in the coil, ensuring the force direction remains the same for continuous rotation.
  5. Carbon brushes maintain electrical contact with the rotating commutator.

Parts of a DC Motor

  • Armature coil — rectangular coil of insulated copper wire (ABCD).
  • Field magnet — strong permanent or electromagnet providing the magnetic field.
  • Split-ring commutator — two half-rings that reverse current every half turn.
  • Carbon brushes — conduct current to the rotating coil.
  • Axle — the shaft on which the coil rotates and delivers mechanical output.

Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's discovery — a changing magnetic field induces electric current.

Faraday's Experiment (1831)

Michael Faraday discovered that when a magnet is moved into or out of a coil, an electric current is induced in the coil. This is called electromagnetic induction.

  • Push magnet into coil → galvanometer deflects in one direction.
  • Pull magnet out of coil → galvanometer deflects in the opposite direction.
  • Magnet stationary (no relative motion) → no deflection (no induced current).
Changing Magnetic Field (or relative motion) → Induced EMF → Induced Current No change in magnetic field = No induced current

Fleming's Right-Hand Rule (Generator Rule)

This rule gives the direction of the induced current when a conductor moves in a magnetic field. Hold your right hand with the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger mutually perpendicular:

  • Thumb → direction of motion of the conductor
  • Forefinger → direction of magnetic field (B)
  • Middle finger → direction of induced current (I)

Factors Affecting Induced Current

  • Greater speed of motion = larger induced current.
  • Stronger magnetic field = larger induced current.
  • More turns in the coil = larger induced current.

Electric Generator

Converting mechanical energy into electrical energy — the reverse of a motor.

How a Generator Works

An electric generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy. It works on the principle of electromagnetic induction: when a coil is rotated in a magnetic field, the changing magnetic flux through the coil induces an EMF (and current).

Domestic Electric Circuits

How electricity is safely distributed and used in our homes.

The Domestic Supply

In India, the domestic electric supply is 220V AC at 50 Hz. The electricity enters our home through three wires:

  • Live wire (red/brown) — carries the current at high voltage (alternating between +220V and -220V).
  • Neutral wire (black/blue) — at approximately zero potential; completes the circuit.
  • Earth wire (green/yellow-green) — safety wire connected to a metal plate buried in the ground.

The potential difference between the live and neutral wires is 220V. All appliances are connected in parallel between the live and neutral wires, so each gets the full voltage and can be independently switched on/off.

Safety Hazards and Prevention

Short Circuit

When the live and neutral wires touch each other directly (due to damaged insulation), the resistance drops to nearly zero, causing a very large current to flow. This can overheat wires and cause fire.

Overloading

When too many high-power appliances are connected to a single circuit, the total current exceeds the safe limit of the wiring. This can also cause overheating and fire.

Fuse

A fuse is a safety device connected in series with the live wire. It contains a thin wire of a low-melting-point alloy. If the current exceeds the rated value, the fuse wire melts and breaks the circuit, preventing damage. The fuse rating should match the appliance (e.g., 5A fuse for a 1000W heater at 220V).

MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker)

Modern alternative to fuses. Automatically trips (switches off) when current exceeds the rated value. Can be reset after the fault is corrected — no need to replace like a fuse wire.

Earthing

The earth wire is connected to the metal body of appliances (like a refrigerator, iron, or geyser). If the live wire accidentally touches the metal body, the current flows through the earth wire to the ground instead of through the person touching the appliance. This prevents electric shock.

Fun Fact

The reason household appliances are connected in parallel (not series) is so that each device gets the full 220V and can be switched on or off independently. If they were in series, switching off one device would cut current to all others!

Domestic Circuit Safety

  • Supply: 220V AC, 50 Hz (Live, Neutral, Earth).
  • Fuse in series with live wire; melts at excess current.
  • MCB: modern resettable alternative to fuse.
  • Earthing protects against electric shock.
  • All appliances connected in parallel.

Key Formulas

  • F = B × I × L (Force on conductor)
  • Fuse rating = Power / Voltage
  • More turns / higher current = stronger field
  • Faster motion / more turns = more induced EMF

Magnetic Field Sources

  • Bar magnet: N to S (outside), S to N (inside).
  • Straight wire: concentric circles (right-hand thumb rule).
  • Solenoid: bar magnet pattern; uniform inside.
  • Electromagnet: solenoid + soft iron core.

Important Scientists

  • Oersted (1820): Current produces magnetic field.
  • Faraday (1831): Electromagnetic induction.
  • Fleming: Left-hand (motor) and right-hand (generator) rules.
  • Ampere: Quantified relationship between current and field.
