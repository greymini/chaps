Chapter Overview

Understanding how light behaves when it bounces off surfaces and bends through different media.

What is Light?

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation that is visible to the human eye. It travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation) at an incredibly fast speed of 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum. When light encounters a surface or passes from one medium to another, two fundamental phenomena occur: reflection and refraction.

This chapter explores how mirrors (curved and plane) and lenses (convex and concave) manipulate light to form images — the physics behind spectacles, cameras, telescopes, and our own eyes.

Reflection of Light

When light strikes a surface, it bounces back into the same medium — this is reflection.

Laws of Reflection

When a ray of light falls on a smooth, polished surface (like a mirror), it is reflected according to two fundamental laws:

Law 1: The angle of incidence (∠i) is always equal to the angle of reflection (∠r).
Law 2: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.

Key Terms

  • Incident ray — The ray of light that falls on the reflecting surface.
  • Reflected ray — The ray of light that bounces off the surface.
  • Normal — An imaginary line perpendicular to the surface at the point of incidence.
  • Angle of incidence (i) — The angle between the incident ray and the normal.
  • Angle of reflection (r) — The angle between the reflected ray and the normal.
  • Point of incidence — The point where the incident ray strikes the surface.

Types of Reflection

  • Regular (Specular) Reflection — Occurs on smooth, polished surfaces (mirrors, calm water). Parallel incident rays remain parallel after reflection, forming a clear image.
  • Diffuse (Irregular) Reflection — Occurs on rough surfaces (walls, paper, unpolished wood). Parallel rays scatter in different directions after reflection, so no clear image is formed. Note: The laws of reflection are still obeyed at each individual point.

Fun Fact

Light travels so fast that it can circle the Earth 7.5 times in one second! At 3 × 10⁸ m/s, light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach us, covering 150 million kilometres.

Spherical Mirrors

Curved mirrors that are part of a sphere — concave (converging) and convex (diverging).

Types of Spherical Mirrors

Concave Mirror: The reflecting surface curves inward (like the inside of a spoon). Parallel rays converge at the focus — hence called a converging mirror.

Convex Mirror: The reflecting surface curves outward (like the back of a spoon). Parallel rays appear to diverge from a virtual focus — hence called a diverging mirror.

Key Terms

  • Pole (P) — The centre of the reflecting surface of the mirror.
  • Centre of Curvature (C) — The centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.
  • Radius of Curvature (R) — The radius of the sphere; the distance from P to C.
  • Principal Axis — The straight line passing through P and C.
  • Principal Focus (F) — The point where rays parallel to the principal axis converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex) after reflection.
  • Focal Length (f) — The distance from P to F.
Relationship: R = 2f The radius of curvature is twice the focal length.

Convex Mirror: Image Formation

A convex mirror always forms an image that is:

  • Virtual (formed behind the mirror)
  • Erect (upright)
  • Diminished (smaller than the object)

This is true regardless of the object’s position. As the object moves closer, the image size increases slightly but remains smaller than the object.

Sign Convention (New Cartesian)

  • All distances are measured from the pole (P) of the mirror.
  • Distances measured in the direction of the incident light (left to right) are positive.
  • Distances measured against the incident light (right to left) are negative.
  • Heights above the principal axis are positive; below are negative.
  • For concave mirrors: u is always −ve, f is −ve. For convex mirrors: f is +ve.
Mirror Formula:
1/v + 1/u = 1/f v = image distance, u = object distance, f = focal length (all in cm or m)
Magnification:
m = h′/h = −v/u m negative → inverted image  |  m positive → erect image  |  |m| > 1 → magnified  |  |m| < 1 → diminished

Refraction of Light

The bending of light when it travels from one transparent medium to another.

What Causes Refraction?

When light passes from one medium to another (e.g., air to glass), it changes speed. This change in speed causes the light ray to change direction — this is refraction.

  • Rarer to denser medium (e.g., air → glass): Light slows down and bends toward the normal.
  • Denser to rarer medium (e.g., glass → air): Light speeds up and bends away from the normal.
  • If light enters perpendicular to the surface (along the normal), it passes through without bending.
Laws of Refraction (Snell’s Law):
n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
or equivalently: sin i / sin r = n₂₁ (refractive index of medium 2 w.r.t. medium 1) i = angle of incidence, r = angle of refraction, n = refractive index

Refractive Index

The refractive index (n) of a medium indicates how much light slows down in that medium compared to vacuum.

n = Speed of light in vacuum / Speed of light in medium = c / v

Key Observation

The emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray but shifted sideways. This shift is called lateral displacement. It depends on the thickness of the slab and the angle of incidence.

Lenses

Transparent optical elements that refract light to converge or diverge it.

Types of Lenses

Convex Lens (Converging): Thick at the centre, thin at the edges. Converges parallel rays to a real focus (F₂).

Concave Lens (Diverging): Thin at the centre, thick at the edges. Diverges parallel rays so they appear to come from a virtual focus (F₁).

Key Terms

  • Optical Centre (O) — The central point of the lens. A ray through O passes undeviated.
  • Principal Focus (F₁, F₂) — Points on either side of the lens where parallel rays converge (convex) or appear to diverge from (concave).
  • Focal Length (f) — Distance from O to F.
  • 2F₁, 2F₂ — Points at twice the focal length on either side.

Concave Lens: Image Formation

A concave lens always forms an image that is:

  • Virtual (on the same side as the object)
  • Erect (upright)
  • Diminished (smaller than the object)

This is true for all positions of the object. The image is always formed between the optical centre and the focus on the same side as the object.

Lens Formula, Magnification & Power

Lens Formula:
1/v − 1/u = 1/f v = image distance, u = object distance, f = focal length
Magnification:
m = h′/h = v/u Positive m → erect image  |  Negative m → inverted image
Power of a Lens:
P = 1/f (where f is in metres) Unit: Dioptre (D). Convex lens → +ve power. Concave lens → −ve power.
Combined Power:
P = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... Powers of individual lenses in contact are simply added algebraically.

Reflection

  • Angle i = Angle r, all in same plane.
  • Regular vs diffuse reflection.
  • Laws hold for curved mirrors too.

Spherical Mirrors

  • Concave: converging, real images (mostly).
  • Convex: diverging, always virtual erect diminished.
  • R = 2f; Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f.

Refraction

  • Caused by change in speed of light.
  • Rarer to denser: bends toward normal.
  • Snell’s Law: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r.

Lenses

  • Convex: converging, +ve power, +ve f.
  • Concave: diverging, −ve power, −ve f.
  • P = 1/f (metres). Unit: Dioptre (D).

Sign Convention Rules

  • Distances from pole/optical centre.
  • Along incident light = +ve.
  • Above axis = +ve, below = −ve.

Key Numerical Tips

  • Always apply sign convention first.
  • Check: m negative = inverted, m positive = erect.
  • Convert f to metres for power calculation.

Key Terms

Reflection — bouncing back of light from a surface
Refraction — bending of light at media boundary
Concave mirror — converging mirror, inward curve
Convex mirror — diverging mirror, outward curve
Principal focus (F) — convergence point of parallel rays
Focal length (f) — distance from pole to focus
Magnification — ratio of image height to object height
Refractive index — ratio of speed in vacuum to speed in medium
Snell’s Law — n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
Convex lens — converging lens, thick at centre
Concave lens — diverging lens, thin at centre
Dioptre (D) — unit of power of a lens
Lateral displacement — sideways shift through glass slab
Virtual image — cannot be caught on screen
Real image — can be caught on screen
